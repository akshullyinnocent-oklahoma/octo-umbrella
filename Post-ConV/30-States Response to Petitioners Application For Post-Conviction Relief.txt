ARGUMENT AND AUTHORITY


I. Petitioner’s Claims are barred by Res Judicata and Waiver


The Post-Conviction Procedure Act, Title 22 O.S. §1080, et seq., is neither a substitute for a direct appeal nor a means for a second appeal. Maines v. State, 1979 OK CR 71, 597 P.2d 774, 775-76; Fox v. State, 1994 OK CR 52, 880 P.2d 383, 384. The scope of this remedial measure is strictly limited and does not allow for litigation of issues available for review at the time of direct appeal. Castro v. State, 1994 OK CR 53, 880 P.2d 387, 388. Issues that were previously raised and ruled upon are procedurally barred from further review under the doctrine of res judicata; issues that were not raised previously on direct appeal, but which could have been raised, are waived for further review. Logan v. State, 2013 OK CR 2, ¶ 3, 293 P.3d 969, 973 (citing 22 O.S. 2001, § 1086; King v. State, 2001 OK CR 22, ¶ 4, 29 P.3d 1089, 1099; Webb v. State, 1992 OK CR 38, ¶ 6, 835 P.2d 115, 116, overruled on other grounds; Neill v. State, 1997 OK CR 41, ¶ 7 n.2, 943 P.2d 145, 148 n.2). “Post-conviction review was neither designed nor intended to provide applicants another direct appeal.” Id.; Cf. Coddington v. State, 2011 OK CR 21, ¶ 2, 259 P.3d 833, 835 (“The post-conviction process is not a second appeal.”).


An exception to this rule exists where a court finds sufficient reason for not asserting or inadequately presenting an issue in prior proceedings or “when an intervening change in constitutional law impacts the judgment and sentence.” Bryson v. State, 1995 OK CR 57, 903 P.2d 333, 334; 22 O.S. 2001 § 1086. Sufficient reason for failing to previously raise or adequately assert an issue requires a showing that some impediment external to the defense prevented the petitioner and counsel from properly raising the claim. Johnson v. State, 1991 OK CR 124, 823 P.2d 370, 373. “Petitioner has the burden of establishing that his alleged claim could not have been previously raised and thus is not procedurally barred." Robinson v. State, 1997 OK CR 24, 937 P.2d 101, 108.


In the present case, each of Petitioner's arguments were, or could have been, raised on direct appeal excluding his claim of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel. Petitioner does not offer this Court any reason, much less one external to the defense, for failing to previously assert these issues. Rather, he acknowledges that they have been waived and blames appellate counsel for not raising it, seeming to really mean that they didn't raise it the way he wanted them to, which does not override the waiver or res judicata implications of reraising these issues in post-conviction as a second appeal. Thus, consideration of these propositions is barred by the doctrine of res judicata and waiver, respectively. The Court of Criminal Appeals has stated that where a claim is procedurally barred, there is no need to address the merits of the issues presented. Boyd v. State, 1996 OK CR 12, 915 P.2d 922, 924. As aptly stated by the Court:


In the case sub judice, Petitioner was afforded an opportunity to pursue a direct appeal; he specifically declined to do so. As a result, he is bound by that earlier decision; as a consequence of that decision, he has forfeited his right to have this Court consider [issues], which would have been readily available for that direct appeal.


Wallace v. State, 1997 OK CR 18, 935 P.2d 366, 370 (citation omitted). Therefore, the allegations of error raised by Petitioner (specifically ineffective assistance of trial counsel, new evidence, and lack of due process) need not be addressed and Petitioner's Application for Post-Conviction Relief should be denied as a matter of law. Nonetheless, Petitioner's claims are wholly without merit.


II. Petitioner’s Ineffective Assistance of Trial and Appellate Counsel Claims Are Without Merit


Petitioner claims that he was constructively denied appellate counsel under United States v. Cronic. That case contemplates assistance of counsel at the trial stage, not the appellate stage,


since the adversarial process is not the same at the appellate stage as the trial stage. Appellate counsel filed a brief and the State responded to that brief and the Court of Criminal Appeals ruled on it. The appeal process was complete at that point, meaning no actual breakdown of the adversarial process. The proper standard to measure assistance of counsel is "reasonably effective assistance". In Strickland v. Washington, 466 U.S. 668, 104 S.Ct. 2052, 80 L.Ed.2d 674 (1984), the Court set out the proper standards for measuring effective assistance of counsel in a two-tier test. First, Petitioner is required to show that counsel's performance was deficient under prevailing professional norms, and that but for the deficient performance the outcome of his case would be different. This requires a showing that counsel made errors so serious that counsel was not functioning as the counsel guaranteed a defendant under the Sixth Amendment. Second, Petitioner must show that the deficient performance prejudiced his defense. This requires showing that counsel's errors were so serious as to deprive a defendant of a fair trial, a trial whose result is reliable. See also Workman v. State, 824 P. 2d, 378 (Okla.Cr. 1991). Furthermore, a defendant must overcome the presumption that, under the circumstances, the challenged action might be considered sound strategy because this court will indulge a strong presumption that counsel's conduct falls within the wide range of reasonable professional assistance." Strickland, 466 U.S. at 689, 104 S.Ct. at 2065.


Examination of the record reveals that Petitioner's arguments fail to overcome the first tier of the Strickland test. Petitioner's trial counsel acted as a reasonable competent attorney under the facts and circumstances of this case. The witnesses were cross-examined. Trial counsel submitted evidence during that cross-examination. Trial counsel objected to questioning or evidence that was potentially injurious to the client. A reviewing court should indulge "a strong presumption that counsel's conduct falls within the wide range of reasonably professional assistance", Strickland.




Moreover, the Court of Criminal Appeals found that the trial counsel’s performance was reasonably competent.


Additionally, Petitioner alleges that trial counsel failed to adequately prepare, have a trial strategy, and call sufficient witnesses. “[W]hen a defendant chooses to have a lawyer manage and present his case, law and tradition may allocate to counsel the power to make binding decisions of trial strategy in many areas.” Faretta v. California, 422 U.S. 806, 820, 95 S.Ct. 2523, 2534, 45 L.Ed. 2d 562 (1975). Included in counsel’s power to make decisions of strategy is the manner and scope of pretrial investigation. Counsel cannot be deemed ineffective for failing to investigate every possible avenue of defense. Browning v. State, 134 P.3d 816, 833 (Oklahoma.Cr. 2006). “An attorney who makes a strategic choice to channel his investigation into fewer than all plausible lines of defense upon which he bases his strategy are reasonable and his choices on the basis of those assumptions are reasonable.” Gilson v. State, 8 P.3d 883, 928 (Oklahoma.Cr. 2000) (quoting Boltz v. State, 806 P.2d 1117, 1126 (Oklahoma.Cr. 1991)). At trial, the determination of what witnesses will be presented, if any, on behalf of the defense is a matter of strategy left to counsel. DeLozier v. State, 991 P.2d 22, 31 (Oklahoma.Cr. 1998). There is a strong presumption that the decision not to present a witness or utilize available evidence is a reasonable trial strategy. Toles v. State, 947 P.2d 180, 188 (Oklahoma.Cr. 1997). So too is the decision to cross-examine witnesses and the manner in which it is done. Wilson v. State, 983 P.2d 448, 457 (Oklahoma.Cr. 1998). While Petitioner challenges the decisions of counsel in these areas of trial strategy, “[t]actical decisions, whether wise or unwise, successful or unsuccessful, cannot ordinarily form the basis for ineffective assistance.” Phillips v. State, 989 P.2d 1017, 1048 (Oklahoma.Cr. 1999).


Petitioner can make no showing of unreasonable performance by counsel and essentially reraises the same argument from the appellant brief he filed. As presented, Petitioner’s challenge


to the efficacy of counsel is nothing more than a conclusory allegation of deficient performance.


Yet, “[c]onclusory allegations, standing alone will never support a finding that an attorney’s performance was deficient. Smith v. State, 955 P.2d 734, 738 (Okla.Cr. 1998), see also, Perry v. State, 853 P.2d 198, 203 (Okla.Cr. 1993) (generalized claim of ineffectiveness for failing to file motions insufficient to meet burden under Strickland); Trice v. State, 912 P.2d 349, 355 n.24 (Okla.Cr. 1996) (“mere allegations of defense counsel’s unpreparedness do not support a claim of ineffective assistance of counsel”); Royal v. State, 839 P.2d 1363, 1373 (Okla.Cr. 1992) (generalized claim of inadequate investigation and preparation and failure to file unspecified motions insufficient to establish claim of ineffective assistance).


Appellate counsel is also held to the Strickland test of reasonably effective assistance. The Oklahoma Court of Criminal Appeals further defined the test in Cartwright v. State, 708 P.2d 592 (Okla. Cr. 1985). The Court held that counsel is not required to advance every argument, regardless of merit. See also Jones v. Barnes, 463 U.S. 745, 103 S.Ct. 3308, 77 L.Ed.2d 987 (1983), and Webb v. State, 835 P.2d 115 (Okla.Cr. 1992). In fact, appellate counsel’s refusal to raise nonfrivolous allegations may be evidence of effectiveness. Banks v. State, 810 P.2d 1286, 1290 (Okla.Cr. 1991), and Nguyen v. State, 844 P.2d 176, 178 (Okla.Cr. 1992). Thus, Petitioner’s argument that the “Numerous deficiencies effectively denied him of a fair and meaningful appeal” is without merit.


Petitioner’s appellate counsel carefully selected legal issues to be raised on appeal, and the Appeals Court denied relief. In Taylor v. State, 659 P.2d 362, 365 (Okla. Cr. 1983), the Court held “Lack of success is not the proper measure for determining the adequacy of legal representation”. Petitioner’s appellate counsel was reasonably competent. Petitioner’s allegation(s) of error fail to establish that the results of petitioner’s direct appeal would have been changed. Petitioner’s allegations equate to appellate counsel (1) constructively denying him the right to counsel under Cronic, (2) not providing the brief to Petitioner before filing, and (3) and failing to cite the record resulting in claims being barred.


First, Petitioner merely states that he was appointed 4 different appellate counsel who did things that together amounted to denial of counsel. However, for this claim to work, we have to (1) suspend the reasonable understanding that attorney have other clients they are working with - especially public defenders, (2) forget about the fact that a brief was filed in the appeal, (3) disregard the striking similarity of argument by Appellate Counsel (who we should believe is ineffective) and the Petitioner (acting pro se), and (4) punish the public defender's office for turnover in their office. None of this makes logical sense or amounts to ineffective assistance of counsel. Failing to provide a copy of the brief before filing does not constitute constitutionally deficient performance of appellate counsel. In fact, it is the purview of appellate counsel to carefully select claims to be presented to the Court to best ensure success. Additionally, claims raised by Petitioner in the present application shed light on the fact that claims Petitioner would have requested be included lacked merit.


Finally, Petitioner claims appellate counsel’s failure to correctly cite all alleged instances of prosecutorial misconduct in closing argument constituted ineffective assistance of appellate counsel. However, a mere procedural failure such as the one raised by the Petitioner is not sufficient to equate appellate counsel’s performance to that of constitutionally deficient representation. As a result, Petitioner’s allegations of ineffective assistance of both trial and appellate counsel must fail.






III. Petitioner’s Factual Innocence Claim is without Merit.


Petitioner’s claim of factual innocence hinges a few points he presents: (1) fabrication of the allegations against him, (2) “newly presented evidence” that doesn’t place him in the house in the timeframe, (3) behavioral evidence that wasn’t raised at trial and was available at that time, (4) conduct of the victim after the touching, (5) false and impeached testimony that the State relied on in it’s case in chief, and (6) the Story of the Victim. Looking at the availability of these things and the rulings made by the Court, the State would submit that Petitioner has not shown factual innocence here but attempts to retry the case with the evidence he would’ve liked to been admitted during the trial. Thus, his factual innocence claim is without merit.


In Bryson v. State, 1997 OK CR 26, 937 P.2d 505, 513 fn. 15, the Court stated that with respect to “actual innocence claims,” a petitioner must show by “clear and convincing evidence” that, but for the alleged error, “no reasonable juror would have found the petitioner guilty of the crime of which he was convicted.” Citing Wallace v. Lockhart, 12 F.3d 823, 827 (8th Cir. 1994). The Court further stated that the “actual innocence” exception is applicable only to factual innocence, where a petitioner can make a colorable showing he is actually innocent of the crime for which he is convicted; it is not applicable to legal innocence. Citations omitted.


The State responded to most, if not all of these claims of factual innocence in the Brief filed with the Court of Criminal Appeals when this case was before them. For this Court to reconsider the factual innocence with evidence not presented during trial and not preserved in the record would be the same miscarriage of justice that Petitioner contends happened to him. Trial counsel likely had this information at trial and deemed it irrelevant to whether or not the allegations the State raised had occurred. In the alternative, it may have been in the strategy of trial counsel not to submit this evidence to not subject them to






It is evident from the exhibits and testimony Petitioner would seek to introduce is neither new evidence, since it was known of at the time of trial, and inadmissible as it is not relevant to the allegations the case hinged on. Moreover, such evidence could not meet the standard of proving actual innocence. After deliberation with all the evidence before them and all the instructions as to the credibility of various witnesses, the 12 members of the jury found Petitioner guilty beyond a reasonable doubt. As a result, Petitioner’s claim should be denied.


IV. Violation of Due Process


Finally, Petitioner alleges that he was denied due process of law because of the evidence deemed inadmissible in his trial and his trial counsel's performance. Each of these issues were raised on direct appeal and affirmed, and addressed within the State's response and shown to be wholly without merit. Additionally, Petitioner has failed to provide this Court with any showing of prejudice beyond mere conclusory statements. For this reason, and those above addressing his underlying claims, Petitioner’s claim should be denied.


V. Petitioner’s Appendix is an attempt to usurp the Order of the Court to Comply with the District Court Rules.


Petitioner filed with his brief an appendix with 107 pages of quotes from Voir Dire and Closing Argument that allege objectionable content and prosecutorial misconduct. The Appendix should be used for exhibits to supplement the brief that the Petitioner has filed, including transcripts and any other exhibits the Petitioner deems necessary. By adding these 107 pages of his own objections, he is not using the appendix properly and attempting to do what his trial counsel already did during the trial. During the trial, there were many opportunities for trial counsel to approach for a bench conference and make a record as to the line of questioning or the comments made during the closing argument. Trial counsel did that and the Court allowed the statement




during closing arguments to rebut the overreaching statements by the Defense in their closing argument. The Appendix also seems to intimate that the Court needs to reverse rulings it made during the trial, which is an odd request, especially when the Court of Criminal Appeals affirmed the rulings and actions by the State in its Summary Opinion. The other exhibits in the Appendix show a lack of understanding of the law and a piecemeal attempt to prove claims that have no legal basis at this juncture.


CONCLUSION


Petitioner offers this Court no sufficient reason to avoid application of the doctrine of waiver and res Judicata as to each claim aside from Ineffective Assistance of Appellate Counsel. Again, multiple times in his brief, he admits that these claims are waived and tries to get around that with an ineffective assistance of appellate counsel argument - which has no merit as the Appellate counsel raised the arguments within the same legal areas in the appeal that was denied by the Court of Criminal Appeals that Petitioner attempts to raise at this juncture. Thus, consideration of these arguments is procedurally barred. Moreover, Petitioner's claims are without merit and should be denied. Finally, in regards to the motions to disqualify, those motions are improper as the Legislature has determined that Post-Conviction matters would be determined by the Sentencing Judge and the District Attorney's Office that prosecuted the case initially. Thus, those motions should be denied as well.

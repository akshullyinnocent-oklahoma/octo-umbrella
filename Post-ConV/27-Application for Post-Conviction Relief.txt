**COMES NOW,** Dallas Christopher Norton, Petitioner pro se, and respectfully submits this Application for Post-Conviction Relief pursuant to 22 O.S. § 1080, 22 O.S § 1080(A)(4) and 22 O.S. § 1081. Petitioner asserts he is being unlawfully held in violation of his constitutional rights under the United States Constitution and the Constitution of the State of Oklahoma. In addition, Petitioner asserts: that there exists evidence of material facts, not previously presented and heard, that requires vacation of the conviction in the interest of justice, pursuant to 22 O.S. § 1080(A)(4).

**JURISDICTION AND LEGAL BASIS**  
This Court has jurisdiction under 22 O.S. § 1080 et seq. Petitioner is currently incarcerated under a judgment and sentence of this Court. The issues presented were not raised on direct appeal through no fault of Petitioner. This Application is timely filed pursuant to 22 O.S. § 1080 and relevant decisions from the Oklahoma Court of Criminal Appeals interpreting its procedural requirement.

**PROCEDURAL HISTORY**  
Petitioner was charged in Cleveland County District Court Case No. CF2019-1273 with 2 Counts of Lewd Acts to a Child Under 16 (21 O.S. § 1123). The case proceeded to jury trial on June 21–24, 2022, in the District Court of Cleveland County. Petitioner was found guilty on both counts. Sentencing was held on August 31, 2022, at which time Petitioner was sentenced to consecutive terms of 25 years, with 7 of Count I and the Entirety of Count II suspended. Petitioner timely filed a direct appeal, Case No. F-2022-746, raising five propositions of error. The Oklahoma Court of Criminal Appeals affirmed the convictions in its Summary Opinion issued on July 3rd, 2024. Petitioner now seeks relief on grounds that were either not previously available or not properly presented due to ineffective assistance of appellate counsel, official misconduct, and newly developed evidence establishing actual innocence.

**FACTUAL BACKGROUND AND STATEMENT OF ACTUAL INNOCENCE**  
The State’s case against the Petitioner rests not on credible evidence, but on a narrative that systematically evolved to overcome exculpatory facts as they emerged. When this inconsistent testimony is measured against suppressed, objective evidence of the Petitioner’s physical location and the complainant’s own documented behavior, the conviction becomes a factual and physical impossibility. The complainant’s account transformed dramatically with each official interview, suggesting a fabricated story rather than the recall of a traumatic event. The initial police report from July 14, 2019, was fundamentally exonerating: the incident allegedly occurred on the living room couch, involved no completed act, and was confined to November–December 2018. Just eight days later, in a forensic interview, the narrative changed entirely. The location moved to the master bedroom, the act escalated to a forced touching, and yet, digital penetration was explicitly denied. By the preliminary hearing nearly six months later, the allegation of digital penetration appeared for the first time, directly contradicting her prior statement.

Throughout these shifting accounts, the one consistent element was the late-2018 timeframe, which the complainant described as “in the middle of fifth grade” and “before last Christmas.” At trial, the State attempted to create ambiguity by focusing on the phrase “before they got married.” However, this theory was demolished on cross-examination when the complainant herself clarified her own understanding, equating the late-2018 separation period with the phrase “before they got married.” The State ignored this dispositive testimony and falsely argued in closing that “Nobody ever said 2018.” This timeline is critical because objective, documentary evidence proves the Petitioner was not present during that period. He signed a new lease on September 4, 2018, and photographic and text message evidence confirm he moved out on September 8, 2018, not returning to reside in the home until December 30, 2018. The complainant, meanwhile, left for a trip to Arizona on December 22, 2018, and did not return until January 5, 2019. There was zero overlap between the parties’ presence in the home during the only timeframe consistently alleged, making the State’s case a physical impossibility.

Furthermore, the complainant’s sworn testimony about her behavior following the alleged incident was proven false by suppressed evidence. She testified under oath that she never went fishing with Petitioner or expressed missing him, yet timestamped text messages from her mother reveal the opposite, describing the complainant as eager for affectionate contact throughout the alleged timeframe. With no medical or physical proof, the State’s case was built entirely upon witnesses whose credibility was either judicially impeached or demonstrably false, including the complainant herself and her father, for whom the court issued inconsistent statement instructions, a detective who gave false testimony under oath, and a forensic interviewer who contradicted her own pretrial admissions about coaching. The jury was deprived of the evidence necessary to see the truth, resulting in the conviction of an innocent man for a crime that was physically impossible to commit.

In 1954, the Oklahoma Court of Criminal Appeals recognized a profound truth at the heart of the American justice system: “The crime charged is so reprehensible that, no matter how innocent a man may be, it is almost impossible for him to get a fair and impartial trial… It is unbelievable to the ordinary layman that a child would concoct such a story unless the same was true… However, courts and attorneys have had ample opportunity to learn that such stories of being molested are often untrue... These observations are, in the opinion of this Court, sound, and demand particular caution and deliberation.” —Davis v. State, 1954 OK CR 54, 28-29.

This long-standing principle of “particular caution and deliberation” was abandoned at every stage of Petitioner’s case. This brief documents a systemic breakdown of the adversarial process—from a premeditated campaign of prosecutorial misconduct that began in voir dire, to a defense counsel who failed to present exculpatory evidence and ultimately attacked his own client, to a series of appellate lawyers who abdicated their duty to challenge these profound errors. This was not merely a trial with errors; it was a proceeding that failed to provide the fundamental fairness demanded by the Constitution, resulting in the wrongful conviction of an innocent man.

Pro se filings should be construed liberally and held to less stringent standards than formal pleadings drafted by lawyers. This Court has a duty to examine pro se pleadings based on the substance of the claims rather than technical form, ensuring that constitutional rights are not forfeited due to lack of legal training. See Haines v. Kerner, 404 U.S. 519, 520 (1972); Hall v. Bellmon, 935 F.2d 1106, 1110 (10th Cir. 1991); Magar v. Parker, 1973 OK CR 394, 515 P.2d 1155, 1156.

**PROPOSITION I: APPELLATE COUNSEL WAS CONSTITUTIONALLY INEFFECTIVE, RESULTING IN A CONSTRUCTIVE DENIAL OF COUNSEL UNDER UNITED STATES v. CRONIC**

**Standard of Review:** Strickland v. Washington, 466 U.S. 668 (1984); United States v. Cronic, 466 U.S. 648 (1984); Evitts v. Lucey, 469 U.S. 387 (1985) (establishing the right to effective assistance of counsel on direct appeal as guaranteed by the Due Process Clause).

This was not a single deficient act, but a cascade of failures so profound as to constitute a constructive denial of counsel under United States v. Cronic, 466 U.S. 648 (1984), where prejudice is presumed when counsel entirely fails to subject the prosecution's case to meaningful adversarial testing. See also Avery v. Alabama, 308 U.S. 444 (1940) (recognizing constructive denial where systemic failures prevent effective representation).

Their omission demonstrates the profound deficiency of an appeal that was, as established in Proposition I, a constructive denial of counsel. Counsel failed to raise numerous, glaring, and meritorious claims that were obvious from the record. In the alternative, several categories of this misconduct were so egregious as to constitute standalone reversible error. See Paxton v. State, 1993 OK CR 59, 15, 867 P.2d 1309, 1318 (repeated vouching for witnesses constitutes plain error warranting reversal). “Full breakdown of EVERY instance detailed in Appendix”

The Campaign Began in Voir Dire: A Systematic Conditioning of the Jury. The prosecution’s premeditated campaign began before the first piece of evidence was ever presented. During a single day of voir dire, the prosecutor engaged in a relentless barrage of more than 185 improper statements and leading questions designed to precondition the jury, neutralize the defense’s strongest arguments, and lower the State’s constitutional burden of proof. Through dozens of leading questions, the prosecutor conditioned the jury to excuse inconsistencies, to accept a lack of corroborating evidence as normal, and to trivialize the "beyond a reasonable doubt" standard with simplistic analogies like "granny's potato soup". (Tr I p119-122+) By the time the first witness was sworn, the presumption of innocence had been systematically eroded.

The Campaign Culminated in a Closing Argument Dominated by Misconduct. The themes initiated in voir dire reached their apex in a closing argument defined by constitutional violations. The Davis court’s warning was prescient. This case is precisely what happens when the system fails to guard against the inherent dangers of such a prosecution.

III. MOTION TO DISQUALIFY JUDGE THAD BALKMAN
Petitioner respectfully moves for the disqualification of Judge Thad Balkman from any proceeding related to his Application for Post-Conviction Relief. Judge Balkman presided over Petitioner's trial and sentencing, and the Application for Post-Conviction Relief challenges specific judicial decisions that Judge Balkman himself made during those proceedings. The fundamental principle of nemo judex in causa sua—that no one should be a judge in his own cause—requires disqualification where a judge is asked to rule on challenges to his own judicial conduct.

A. Allowing the admission of unreliable hearsay without proper review of the forensic interview that contained material inconsistencies; Judge Balkman allowed the admission of unreliable hearsay without conducting a proper review of the forensic interview that contained material inconsistencies. 

The forensic interview, which forms the basis for the hearsay admission under 12 O.S. § 2803.1, shows the complainant making statements that evolved significantly from her initial disclosure and that contradict her trial testimony on critical points (APCR Exhibits B1 through B16), including the complainant's statement that the alleged acts occurred "before they were married" (APCR Exhibit B3 Forensic Interview Tr.), her denial that anything occurred while she was on her back (APCR Exhibit B4 Forensic Interview Tr.), and her placement of events in her mother's bedroom (APCR Exhibit B13 Forensic Interview Tr.) rather than on the living room couch as she later testified at trial. Furthermore, Judge Balkman failed to comply with the express procedural mandates of 12 O.S. § 2803.1(B) regarding the admission of child hearsay. Under this statute, a judge is required to hold a hearing and find that the hearsay statement possesses "particularized guarantees of trustworthiness" before it can be admitted. The transcript of the reliability hearing (APCR Exhibit L6 Reliability Hearing Tr. p. 6) confirms that Judge Balkman did not watch the forensic interview video—the evidence he was being asked to admit—contemporaneously with the legal arguments. The court stated on the record: "I'll take the arguments under advisement, and once I watch the interview that's Exhibit 1”, (which did not occur until several months later during the trial) “I'll be able to make a determination".

This separation of the evidence from the adversarial testing process rendered the hearing statutorily deficient. By failing to review the video in the presence of counsel where specific inconsistencies could be highlighted, the judge bypassed the "meaningful inquiry" required by Idaho v. Wright, 497 U.S. 805 (1990). The record further substantiates that during this same hearing, the forensic interviewer, Christi Cornett, admitted that the complainant "clearly had been told or informed as to what to expect" (APCR Exhibit L1 Reliability Hearing Tr. p. 12). Despite this admission of coaching by the State’s own expert, Judge Balkman admitted the statement as "reliable". A judge who has already certified coached testimony as reliable is structurally biased against a post-conviction claim that the admission of that testimony violated the Confrontation Clause.

B. Permitting Detective Judy's false testimony about victim contact to stand uncorrected despite direct contradiction by the victim's sworn VPO testimony; Judge Balkman permitted Detective Sean Judy's false testimony regarding victim contact to stand uncorrected despite direct contradiction by the victim's own previous testimony. Detective Judy testified at trial that he "can't talk to her" and had no contact with the complainant (APCR Exhibit G1 Trial Tr. Vol. III p. 45). The transcript of the July 29th 2019 Protection Order Hearing reveals that the complainant testified she had "talked to Det[ective]" and "gave statements" (APCR Exhibit C9 VPO Tr. p. 8) and that she "talked to Police and Detective" (APCR Exhibit C10 VPO Tr. p. 14). This false testimony went uncorrected despite its materiality to the reliability of the investigation and the credibility of the State's case.

C. Failing to intervene during numerous instances of prosecutorial misconduct in closing arguments, including improper vouching, inflammatory rhetoric, burden-shifting, and misstatements of law and fact; Judge Balkman failed to intervene during a systematic campaign of prosecutorial misconduct that pervaded both voir dire and closing arguments. The Application for Post-Conviction Relief documents 285+ specific instances of misconduct with precise page and line citations to the trial transcripts included in its appendix. During voir dire, the prosecutor engaged in more than 185 improper statements and leading questions designed to precondition the jury, neutralize the defense's strongest arguments, and lower the State's constitutional burden of proof. These violations included trivializing the reasonable doubt standard through analogies such as "granny's potato soup," improperly defining legal terms that are the exclusive province of the court, and attempting to pre-commit jurors to specific conclusions before hearing any evidence. - The misconduct reached its apex during closing arguments, where the State committed no fewer than 85 documented instances of constitutional violations. Most egregiously, the prosecutor personally vouched for witness credibility on multiple occasions, stating "we know he's not lying" (APCR Exhibit H Trial Tr. Vol. IV p. 65 lines 12-13) in direct defiance of the court's OUJI-CR 9-20 instruction that the witnesses were potentially "not believable or truthful." The prosecutor also used inflammatory language, calling Petitioner a "child molester" (APCR Exhibit H Trial Tr. Vol. IV p. 93 lines 4-5), and made demonstrably false statements about the evidence, claiming "No one ever said 2018" (APCR Exhibit H Trial Tr. Vol. IV p. 95 lines 5-6) when Officer Pierce's police report explicitly documented dates of "11/29/2018-12/25/2018" (APCR Exhibit A2 Police Rpt. p. 1) and the complainant consistently testified to events occurring "before Christmas" of 2018 (APCR Exhibits C3, C5, C6 VPO Tr.).

D. Denying Petitioner's motion for new trial despite clear grounds under 22 O.S. § 952(4)-(7), including the prosecutor's own admission during closing that certain hearsay evidence was "not reliable";  The motion was supported by contemporaneous documentation, including a letter Petitioner sent to the court Immediately after the trial (APCR Exhibits I1, I2, I3) detailing how trial counsel had coerced him into waiving his right to testify during a lunch break in the middle of trial, and through a rushed bench conference conducted in whispers because the Jury was present. This is indicated in the transcripts by Mr. Douglas saying - "I'll speak as low as I can" and Ms. Austin interrupted to say - “Kind of getting a little loud” (APCR Exhibit L2, L3 Trial Tr. Vol. IV p. 12). 

During a New Trial Motion hearing immediately prior to sentencing Mr. Nedwick attacked Petitioner’s credibility when he attempted to testify about the Coercion by stating “I can't allow him to testify to something I know is false” “...is absolutely false” and "Not in my opinion.. Factually" (APCR Exhibits I4, I8 Trial Tr. Vol. V p. 10-11). 

Despite Ms. Austin herself admitting during closing arguments that Jeff Jackson's testimony which directly contradicted all other versions (and had been admitted erroneously by Judge Balkman under the hearsay exception as having a certain ‘indica of reliability”) was "not reliable," after previously arguing for its admittance on the basis of reliability under 2803.1, 
Judge Balkman denied the motion without addressing his error or the other substantial grounds for relief.

E. Failing to properly instruct the jury on critical issues, including no corrective instruction after the prosecutor improperly defined reasonable doubt;
Judge Balkman failed to give a corrective jury instruction after the prosecutor improperly defined "reasonable doubt" during voir dire. The prosecutor told prospective jurors that reasonable doubt "is not beyond all doubt; it's not beyond a shadow of a doubt" (APCR App. #171 Trial Tr. Vol I p. 186) and used the "granny's potato soup" analogy to trivialize this critical legal standard and elements of a crime (APCR App. #7 Trial Tr. Vol. I p. 119-122). Despite defense counsel's objection, no corrective instruction was given, leaving the jury with an improper understanding of the State's burden of proof.

Legal Basis for Disqualification

The principle of nemo judex in causa sua—"no one should be a judge in his own cause"—is a fundamental tenet of natural justice and constitutional due process in American jurisprudence. Calder v. Bull, 3 U.S. 386, 388 (1798). The United States Supreme Court mandated recusal where a judge had significant prior involvement in a case, holding that "an unconstitutional potential for bias exists when the same person serves as both accuser and adjudicator." Williams v. Pennsylvania, 579 U.S. 1, 8-9 (2016). Here, Judge Balkman is both the decision-maker whose conduct is being challenged and the adjudicator who must determine whether that conduct was proper. This dual role creates precisely the unconstitutional potential for bias that Williams prohibits

IV. MOTION TO DISQUALIFY JUDGE LORI PUCKETT
Petitioner respectfully moves for the disqualification of Judge Lori Puckett from any involvement in proceedings related to his Application for Post-Conviction Relief. Judge Puckett presided over Petitioner's preliminary hearing on January 15, 2021, and the Application for Post-Conviction Relief challenges specific judicial decisions that Judge Puckett made during that proceeding.
A. Binding Petitioner over for trial despite inconsistent and factually impossible allegations, in violation of 22 O.S. § 258, OUJI-CR 9-20, and the Due Process Clauses of the U.S. and Oklahoma Constitutions;
Judge Puckett bound Petitioner over for trial despite testimony that rendered the allegations physically impossible. The complainant’s testimony at the preliminary hearing consistently anchored the alleged events to the "middle of fifth grade" (APCR Exhibit D4 PHC Tr. p. 22) and specifically "not in 4th grade" (APCR Exhibit D11 PHC Tr. p. 30). However, evidence established that Petitioner moved out of the home in the summer of 2018 and did not reside there during the timeframe specified by the complainant (APCR Exhibit D16 PHC Tr. p. 68). By ignoring the physical impossibility created by the State’s own witness testimony, Judge Puckett failed to perform her gatekeeping function under 22 O.S. § 258.
B. Permitting arbitrary expansion of the timeframe to nearly two years despite testimony narrowing the allegation to a period of less than 25 days when Petitioner demonstrably did not reside in the home;
Judge Puckett allowed the State to utilize a broad, two-year timeframe in the charging documents despite the complainant’s own sworn testimony narrowing the window to a specific period in late 2018. By permitting the State to bypass the "middle of fifth grade" window established by the witness, Judge Puckett facilitated a "moving target" prosecution that prevented Petitioner from mounting an effective defense based on his actual residence.
C. Allowing the addition of Count II based on an evolved version of the allegation that contradicted prior statements, in violation of 22 O.S. §§ 258, 304; 21 O.S. § 1123, and Petitioner's due process right to notice and fair trial; Judge Puckett permitted the addition of a second count involving penetration despite the fact that this allegation appeared for the first time eighteen months after the initial disclosure (APCR Exhibit D7 PHC Tr. p. 40). Prior statements in the police report (APCR Exhibit A2 Police Rpt. p. 3), the forensic interview (APCR Exhibits B1-B2 Forensic Interview Tr.), and the VPO hearing (APCR Exhibits C1-C16 VPO Tr.) contained no mention of such acts.
D. Denying the Motion to Quash despite self-impeaching testimony that narrowed the timeline to a period during which Petitioner was not living in the home; Judge Puckett denied the Motion to Quash even after the State’s own evidence established the physical impossibility of the charges. During the preliminary hearing, the State’s witnesses and the prosecutor herself acknowledged that Petitioner "moved out Summer 18" (APCR Exhibit D6 PHC Tr. p. 45) and "never spent [the] night" at the residence during the alleged timeframe (APCR Exhibit F13 PHC Tr. p. 85).
E. Accepting Detective Judy's constitutionally deficient investigation that violated fundamental investigative protocols; Judge Puckett accepted and relied upon a fundamentally flawed investigative record as the basis for binding Petitioner over for trial. The record reflected a narrative that significantly "evolved" only after a meeting with Ms. Austin. The original police report (APCR Exhibit A2 Police Rpt.) and the forensic interview (July 22, 2019) both contained explicit denials of a second act and penetration.
Legal Basis for Disqualification
The same constitutional principles that require Judge Balkman's disqualification apply with equal force to Judge Puckett. She cannot impartially adjudicate the claim that this deficient investigation violated Petitioner’s right to Due Process because her own judicial oversight of these investigative standards is now being challenged.

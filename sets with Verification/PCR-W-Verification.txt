GROUND 45: VERBATIM ADOPTION OF STATE'S BRIEF

Issue: Whether the district court violated due process by issuing a GROUND : order that was a near-verbatim copy of the State's response, containing no independent findings, no analysis, and no engagement with Petitioner's evidence.

Rule: A post-conviction order "predicated ex parte on findings and conclusions drafted by the prosecution" undermines judicial integrity and violates due process. Pederson v. State, 649 N.W.2d 161, 167 (Minn. 2002). Federal courts have held that "wholesale adoptions" of a party's memoranda "provide an inadequate and inappropriate basis for meaningful determination" and appellate review. Rudenko v. Costello, 286 F.3d 51, 62-63 (2d Cir. 2002). In the Tenth Circuit, "to dispose of a claim without considering the facts supporting it is not a decision on the merits." Wilson v. Workman, 577 F.3d 1284, 1295 (10th Cir. 2009). A state court decision grounded in procedural principles without independent analysis is not entitled to AEDPA deference. Thomas v. Warden, 786 F.3d 450, 459 (6th Cir. 2015).

Facts: On January 9, 2026, the State filed its Response to Petitioner's Application for Post-Conviction Relief. The State's brief was a seven-page document arguing procedural bars and asserting Petitioner's claims were "conclusory" and "without merit." On February 13, 2026, the district court issued its Order Denying Petitioner's Application for Post-Conviction Relief. A side-by-side comparison reveals the court's order is a near-verbatim reproduction of the State's response. The order replicates the State's language on procedural bars, the State's mischaracterization of Petitioner's claims as "conclusory," the State's false assertions that "each of Petitioner's arguments were, or could have been, raised on direct appeal," and the State's argument that Petitioner's evidence was "not new" and "not relevant." The order contains no original findings of fact, no original conclusions of law, no citations to Petitioner's 171 exhibits, and no indication the court independently reviewed the record. Petitioner had filed a Notice of Admissions on January 16, 2026, alerting the court to the State's factual falsehoods; the court adopted them anyway. The OCCA summarily affirmed.

Analysis: The verbatim order was not a judicial act; it was a clerical transcription of the State's litigation position. Under Pederson, when a post-conviction order is "predicated ex parte on findings and conclusions drafted by the prosecution," it undermines the appearance of justice. Under Rudenko, "wholesale adoptions" provide an "inadequate and inappropriate basis for meaningful determination." The Tenth Circuit in Wilson held that a court's failure to consider the evidence presented means it "has not adjudicated that claim on the merits." Here, the court adopted the State's false assertions—that Petitioner "acknowledges that his claims have been waived," that all claims were raised on direct appeal, that the evidence was "not new"—without any independent verification. The order ignored 171 exhibits, a 107-page appendix, and the undisputed timeline evidence proving physical impossibility. The OCCA's affirmance of this nullity was not a merits adjudication entitled to AEDPA deference. Petitioner was denied the independent judicial review that due process requires.

Conclusion: The verbatim adoption of the State's brief violated due process and was not a merits adjudication. The conviction must be vacated, and this Court should review all claims de novo.

—

GROUND 46: FACTUAL ERRORS CONTRADICTED BY RECORD

Issue: Whether the district court's denial order contained factual findings that were demonstrably false and contradicted by the record, including the false claims that Petitioner's claims were raised on direct appeal and that Petitioner acknowledged waiver.

Rule: State court factual findings "might represent an 'unreasonable determination of the facts' where... reasonable minds could not disagree that the trial court misapprehended or misstated material aspects of the record in making its finding." Cardoza v. Rock, 731 F.3d 169, 196 (2d Cir. 2013) (citing Wiggins v. Smith, 539 U.S. 510, 528 (2003)). A factual determination is unreasonable if it is "arbitrary" or if "no fairminded jurist" could agree with it. Sims v. Hyatte, 914 F.3d 1078, 1084 n.1 (7th Cir. 2018) (citing Wood v. Allen, 558 U.S. 290, 301 (2010)). A state court's refusal to credit evidence that "any reasonable adjudicator would have credited" is an unreasonable determination of the facts. Brumfield v. Cain, 576 U.S. 1, 13 (2015).

Facts: The district court's order stated: "Each of Petitioner's arguments were, or could have been, raised on direct appeal" (Order, pp. 2, 8). The order further stated: "Petitioner acknowledges that his claims have been waived" (Order, p. 4). Both statements are categorically false. Petitioner's application raised claims of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel (IAAC) under Cronic and Strickland, factual innocence, and cumulative error. IAAC claims cannot be raised on direct appeal; post-conviction is the first available forum. See 22 O.S. § 1089(D)(4)(b)(2). Factual innocence was supported by "evidence of material facts, not previously presented and heard" under 22 O.S. § 1080(D)—including lease documents, text messages, photographs, and prior sworn testimony never presented to the jury—making it unreachable on direct appeal. Petitioner never acknowledged waiver; the application explicitly invoked IAAC as "sufficient reason" under § 1086 to overcome any procedural bar. The district court also stated that Petitioner's claims were "conclusory" (Order, p. 6), a finding directly contradicted by the 171 exhibits and 107-page appendix filed in support. The OCCA repeated these errors in its summary affirmance.

Analysis: These were not mere errors—they were objectively false statements contradicted by Petitioner's own filings. Under Cardoza, a finding is unreasonable when "reasonable minds could not disagree that the trial court misapprehended or misstated material aspects of the record." The order misstated that Petitioner acknowledged waiver when he explicitly invoked IAAC as sufficient reason. It found all claims were raised on direct appeal when IAAC and factual innocence were not. Under Sims and Wood, no "fairminded jurist" could agree that a petitioner who invoked IAAC as "sufficient reason" had "acknowledged waiver." Under Brumfield, the state court's refusal to credit Petitioner's explicit procedural invocation—evidence any reasonable adjudicator would have credited—was unreasonable. The false findings formed the sole basis for the application of procedural bars.

Conclusion: The district court's factually false denial order violated due process. The conviction must be vacated.

—

GROUND 47: DISMISSAL OF DOCUMENTED CLAIMS AS "CONCLUSORY"

Issue: Whether the district court's dismissal of Petitioner's 171 exhibits and 107-page appendix as "conclusory" and "bald assertions" was an unreasonable determination of facts in violation of due process.

Rule: A state court's dismissal of evidence-backed claims as "conclusory" is permissible only when the allegations are "truly bare and contradicted by the record, not when the record itself contains the evidence the court allegedly ignored." Gray v. Zook, 806 F.3d 783, 796 (4th Cir. 2015). A factual determination is unreasonable if "no fairminded jurist could conclude the determination was, in fact, correct." Smith v. Aldridge, 904 F.3d 874, 882 (10th Cir. 2018) (citing Brumfield v. Cain, 576 U.S. 1, 13 (2015)). A state court's refusal to grant an evidentiary hearing or credit evidence "that any reasonable adjudicator would have credited" is an unreasonable determination of the facts. Brumfield, 576 U.S. at 13.

Facts: Petitioner submitted 171 exhibits with his application, including 102 pages of transcript excerpts, 19 appeal documents, 16 pages of VPO transcript, and 36 photographs and messages. The exhibits documented: trial counsel's on-record admission of lack of preparation (Ex. G12), the coerced waiver colloquy (Ex. L2-L3), the prosecutor's false statement "No one ever said 2018" (Ex. H2), the State's knowing use of false testimony from Detective Judy, Tiffany Norton, Bailey Ferguson, and Christi Cornett (Ex. C9-C10, G1, J27-J29, L1, G10), the destruction of exculpatory BWC evidence (Ex. A5, F17-F18, G3-G6), and the complete breakdown of representation at sentencing where counsel branded his own client a liar (Ex. I1-I10). The 107-page appendix catalogued over 265 instances of prosecutorial misconduct with specific page and line citations. The district court dismissed all of this as "conclusory" without addressing a single exhibit. The OCCA affirmed.

Analysis: Under Gray, a court may dismiss claims as "conclusory" only when the allegations are "truly bare and contradicted by the record." Here, the record contained 171 exhibits with specific transcript citations, photographs, and messages. The allegations were not bare; they were documented. Under Smith (Tenth Circuit), "no fairminded jurist" could conclude that 171 exhibits and a 107-page appendix with specific citations are "conclusory." The court did not merely reach a different conclusion—it failed to engage with the evidence at all. The court's dismissal of a coerced waiver claim supported by a contemporaneous letter and transcript, an unpreparedness claim supported by counsel's own on-record admission, and an innocence claim supported by documentary proof of physical impossibility as "conclusory" defies logic.

Conclusion: The district court's dismissal of documented claims as "conclusory" violated due process. The conviction must be vacated.

—

GROUND 48: FAILURE TO HOLD EVIDENTIARY HEARING

Issue: Whether the district court violated due process by denying Petitioner's post-conviction application without an evidentiary hearing despite material factual disputes supported by 171 exhibits.

Rule: A federal habeas petitioner is entitled to an evidentiary hearing if he asserts a factual basis that, if true, would entitle him to relief, and he was diligent in developing the claim in state court. Boyle v. McKune, 544 F.3d 1132, 1135 (10th Cir. 2008). A hearing is constitutionally required when a petitioner alleges facts that are not refuted by the record and would entitle him to relief. Townsend v. Sain, 372 U.S. 293, 312 (1963). Oklahoma law independently mandates a hearing when material facts are disputed. 22 O.S. § 1084 ("When material facts are disputed, the court shall conduct an evidentiary hearing").

Facts: Petitioner's application presented claims supported by 171 exhibits and a 107-page appendix. These exhibits created material factual disputes on claims of coerced waiver of the right to testify, trial counsel's conflict of interest and public contradiction of Petitioner at sentencing, the State's bad-faith destruction of exculpatory evidence, the State's knowing use of false testimony, appellate counsel's constructive denial of counsel, and Petitioner's factual innocence based on documentary proof of physical impossibility. The State's response did not contest any of these material facts; it relied exclusively on procedural arguments. Under Rule 13(e) of the Rules for District Courts of Oklahoma, the State's failure to dispute material facts constituted an admission. The district court denied the application without holding an evidentiary hearing, without examining the exhibits, and without making independent factual findings. Its order was a verbatim copy of the State's proposed order.

Analysis: Under Townsend, when a petitioner alleges facts that, if proven, would entitle him to relief, a hearing is required. Under Boyle, Petitioner was entitled to a hearing because he alleged facts "not refuted by the record and would entitle him to relief." The 171 exhibits created clear factual disputes that could not be resolved on the record alone—particularly the claims of coerced waiver, which required credibility determinations between Petitioner and his former counsel, and the claims of factual innocence, which required evaluation of documentary evidence never presented to the jury. Under Oklahoma law, an evidentiary hearing is mandatory when material facts are disputed. 22 O.S. § 1084. By refusing to hold a hearing, the court denied Petitioner any opportunity to prove his factual allegations. The OCCA's affirmance of this procedural failure was unreasonable under § 2254(d)(1).

Conclusion: The denial of an evidentiary hearing violated due process. Petitioner is entitled to an evidentiary hearing in this Court, and the conviction must be vacated.

—

GROUND 49: FAILURE TO DISQUALIFY JUDGE BALKMAN

Issue: Whether Judge Balkman's refusal to recuse himself from adjudicating claims that directly challenged his own judicial conduct violated due process.

Rule: A judge cannot impartially rule on claims alleging his own constitutional errors. Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co., 556 U.S. 868, 886 (2009) ("An objective inquiry requires recusal when the probability of actual bias on the part of the judge is too high to be constitutionally tolerable"). "A fair trial in a fair tribunal is a basic requirement of due process." In re Murchison, 349 U.S. 133, 136 (1955) ("No man can be a judge in his own case"). A constitutionally intolerable risk of actual bias exists when the same person serves as both the subject of a constitutional challenge and the adjudicator of that challenge. Williams v. Pennsylvania, 136 S. Ct. 1899, 1905 (2016).

Facts: Petitioner moved to disqualify Judge Balkman on August 11, 2025. The motion detailed Judge Balkman's personal involvement in multiple claims raised in the application: the failure to conduct a proper waiver colloquy, the predetermined ruling on the new-trial motion, the failure to correct the prosecutor's false statement to the jury, the failure to give a limiting instruction for Ferguson's testimony, the admission of an unauthenticated substitute forensic interview disk, the unconstitutional PSI, and the verbatim adoption of the State's brief. Judge Balkman denied the motion on February 4, 2026, in a two-page order dismissing Petitioner's claims as "bare allegations." He then proceeded to rule on the application, denying all claims in the verbatim order issued nine days later. No other judge reviewed the disqualification motion. The OCCA affirmed without addressing the disqualification issue.

Analysis: The principle that no person should be judge in his own cause is fundamental to due process. Under Caperton, an objective inquiry requires recusal when the probability of actual bias is too high to be constitutionally tolerable. Under Murchison, "no man can be a judge in his own case." Under Williams, there is an impermissible risk of bias when a judge who was personally involved in critical decisions is later asked to review the propriety of those decisions. Here, the application directly challenged Judge Balkman's own conduct at trial and at the sentencing/new-trial hearing. He was asked to rule on whether his own waiver colloquy was constitutionally adequate, whether his own PSI was unconstitutional, and whether his own failure to correct the prosecutor's false statement violated due process. His personal interest in the outcome of claims challenging his own judicial integrity created a constitutionally intolerable conflict requiring disqualification. The subsequent verbatim adoption of the State's brief—without independent analysis—confirms the very bias the disqualification motion sought to prevent.

Conclusion: Judge Balkman's failure to disqualify himself violated due process. The conviction must be vacated.

—

GROUND 50: FAILURE TO ISSUE FINDINGS OF FACT AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW

Issue: Whether the district court's failure to issue any findings of fact or conclusions of law—as required by OCCA Rule 5.2(A) and 22 O.S. § 1084—violated due process and frustrated meaningful appellate review.

Rule: A state court's failure to comply with its own procedural rules does not, in itself, raise a federal constitutional claim, but where that failure results in a decision that is fundamentally arbitrary or prevents a petitioner from obtaining meaningful appellate review, it can violate due process. Wilson v. Sellers, 138 S. Ct. 1188, 1192 (2018) ("Federal courts 'look through' unexplained state-court decisions, which are not entitled to AEDPA deference"). Oklahoma law mandates that a court "shall make specific findings of fact, and state expressly its conclusions of law, relating to each issue presented." 22 O.S. § 1084. "Fundamental fairness requires that the district court enter specific findings of fact and conclusions of law relating to each material issue." DeLozier v. State, 2007 OK CR 34, ¶ 56, 173 P.3d 78, 79. A decision that contains no reasoning or factual findings is not an "adjudication on the merits" entitled to deference. Wilson, 138 S. Ct. at 1192.

Facts: The district court's February 13, 2026 order contained zero original findings of fact and zero original conclusions of law. It was a near-verbatim copy of the State's response. It did not identify which materials were considered, did not explain why any claim was rejected, did not cite a single exhibit, did not apply any legal standard to any factual allegation, and did not provide any reasoning for its conclusions. The order simply declared the claims "barred" and "without merit" without analysis. The OCCA was presented with a judicial nullity rather than a reasoned decision, and it affirmed without addressing the structural defect.

Analysis: The complete absence of findings rendered meaningful appellate review impossible. Under Wilson, such an unexplained decision is not entitled to AEDPA deference, and this Court must review the claims de novo. Under DeLozier, the failure to provide specific findings relating to each material issue is structural error. The OCCA's rules require findings of fact and conclusions of law precisely to enable meaningful review. Here, there was nothing to review—no explanation of why 171 exhibits were insufficient, no reasoning on why the innocence claim failed, no analysis of the IAAC claim under either Cronic or Strickland. The state court's failure to follow its own mandatory procedural rule resulted in a fundamentally arbitrary outcome that deprived Petitioner of a fair post-conviction process. The order is a legal nullity that cannot serve as the basis for denying relief.

Conclusion: The district court's failure to issue findings of fact and conclusions of law violated due process. The conviction must be vacated.

ORDER DENYING PETITIONER'S APPLICATION FOR POST-CONVICTION RELIEF

On June 30, 2025, the Petitioner filed his initial Post Conviction Relief Application, which exceeded the page limit. The Court ordered the Petitioner to conform to the Rules of the District Court on July 24, 2025. On October 7, 2025, Petitioner filed his next application and brief together with an appendix comprised of 107 pages of quotes from Voir Dire and Closing Argument that allege objectionable content and prosecutorial misconduct.

An appendix contains exhibits supplementing the brief, including transcripts and any other exhibits deemed necessary to support the application and referenced in the brief. The Petitioner's appendix is styled as an appendix but includes his objections to what he claims his trial counsel should have done at trial. However, during the trial, there were many opportunities for trial counsel to approach for a bench conference and make a record as to the line of questioning or the comments made during the closing argument. Petitioner's trial counsel did that, and the Court
allowed the State, during closing argument, to rebut the statements by the Defense in their closing argument. The appendix also suggests that the Court must reverse rulings it made during the trial. However, the Court of Criminal Appeals has already affirmed the rulings and actions by the State in its Summary Opinion. By adding more than 100 pages of his own objections, Petitioner is not using the appendix properly and attempting to do what his trial counsel already did during the trial.

The Petitioner's appendix is in violation of the District Court Rule No. 16(C) limiting the number of pages, and could be stricken or not considered pursuant to Rule 16(G), however, the Court will address the merits of Petitioner's Application.
The Petitioner submitted the instant Application for Post-Conviction Relief on October 7, 2025, raising the following claims of error:

 * A: Appellate Counsel was constitutionally ineffective, resulting in a constructive denial of counsel under United States v. Cronic.
 * B: In the Alternative, Appellate Counsel's Deficient Performance in Failing to Raise Numerous Meritorious Claims Requires Reversal Under Strickland v. Washington.
 * C: The Cumulative Effect of Appellate Counsel's Numerous Deficiencies Deprived Petitioner of a Fair and Meaningful Direct Appeal.
 * D: Petitioner is Factually Innocent.
 * E: Petitioner's "Appendix".

The Post-Conviction Procedure Act, Title 22 O.S. §1080, et seq., is neither a substitute for a direct appeal nor a means for a second appeal. Maines v. State, 1979 OK CR 71, 597 P.2d 774, 775-76; Fox v. State, 1994 OK CR 52, 880 P.2d 383, 384. The Post-Conviction Procedure Act is strictly limited and does not allow for litigation of issues available for review at the time of direct appeal. Castro v. State, 1994 OK CR 53, 880 P.2d 387, 388.

The doctrine of res judicata bars review of issues that were previously raised and ruled upon; issues that were not raised previously on direct appeal, but which could have been raised, are waived for further review. Logan v. State, 2013 OK CR 2, 3, 293 P.3d 969, 973 (citing 22 O.S. 2001, § 1086; King v. State, 2001 OK CR 22, 14, 29 P.3d 1089, 1090; Webb v. State, 1992 OK CR 38, 6, 835 P.2d 115, 116, overruled on other grounds; Neill v. State, 1997 OK CR 41, 7 n.2, 943 P.2d 145, 148 n.2).

An exception to this rule exists where a court finds sufficient reason for not asserting or inadequately presenting an issue in prior proceedings or "when an intervening change in constitutional law impacts the judgment and sentence." Bryson v. State, 1995 OK CR 57, 903 P.2d 333, 334; 22 O.S. 2001 § 1086. Sufficient reason for failing to previously raise or adequately assert an issue requires a showing that some impediment external to the defense prevented the petitioner and counsel from properly raising the claim. Johnson v. State, 1991 OK CR 124, 823 P.2d 370, 373. "Petitioner has the burden of establishing that his alleged claim could not have been previously raised and thus is not procedurally barred." Robinson v. State, 1997 OK CR 24, 937 P.2d 101, 108.

In the instant case, each of Petitioner's arguments were, or could have been, raised on direct appeal, excluding his claim of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel. Petitioner does not offer this Court any reason for failing to previously assert these issues. Petitioner acknowledges that his claims have been waived and attempts to assign blame to his appellate counsel. These are not grounds to override the waiver or res judicata implications of reraising these issues in post-conviction as a second appeal. Therefore, consideration of Petitioner's propositions of error are barred by the doctrine of res judicata and waiver.

The Court of Criminal Appeals has stated that where a claim is procedurally barred, there is no need to address the merits of the issues presented. Boyd v. State, 1996 OK CR 12, 915 P.2d 922, 924. The Court stated:

> In the case sub judice, Petitioner was afforded an opportunity to pursue a direct appeal; he specifically declined to do so. As a result, he is bound by that earlier decision; as a consequence of that decision, he has forfeited his right to have this Court consider [issues], which would have been readily available for that direct appeal. Wallace v. State, 1997 OK CR 18, 935 P.2d 366, 370 Therefore, the allegations of error, specifically ineffective assistance of trial counsel, new evidence, and lack of due process raised by Petitioner are denied as a matter of law. However, this Court also finds that Petitioner's claims are without merit. Petitioner claims that he was constructively denied appellate counsel under United States v. Cronic. Appellate counsel filed a brief and the State responded to that brief, and the Court of Criminal Appeals ruled on it; there was no actual breakdown of the adversarial process. The proper standard to measure assistance of counsel is "reasonably effective assistance". In Strickland v. Washington, 466 U.S. 668, 104 S.Ct. 2052, 80 L.Ed. 2d 674 (1984), the United States Supreme Court set out the proper standards for measuring effective assistance of counsel in a two-tier test.

First, Petitioner is required to show that counsel's performance was deficient under prevailing professional norms, and that but for the deficient performance, the outcome of his case would be different. This requires a showing that counsel made errors so serious that counsel was not functioning as the counsel guaranteed a defendant under the Sixth Amendment.
Second, Petitioner must show that the deficient performance prejudiced his defense. This requires showing that counsel's errors were so serious as to deprive a defendant of a fair trial, a trial whose result is reliable. See also Workman v. State, 824 P. 2d. 378 (Okl. Cr. 1991). Furthermore, a defendant must overcome the presumption that, under the circumstances, the challenged action might be considered sound strategy because this court will indulge a strong presumption that counsel's conduct falls within the wide range of reasonable professional assistance." Strickland, 466 U.S. at 689, 104 S.Ct. at 2065.

A thorough review of the record reveals that Petitioner's arguments fail to overcome the first prong of the Strickland test. Petitioner's trial counsel was competent and acted as a reasonable competent attorney under the facts and circumstances of this case. Trial counsel cross-examined witnesses and submitted evidence during that cross-examination. Trial counsel raised objections to questions or evidence that was potentially prejudicial to the client. This court indulges "a strong presumption that counsel's conduct falls within the wide range of reasonably professional assistance", Strickland.

Petitioner's allegations that trial counsel failed to adequately prepare, have a trial strategy, and call sufficient witnesses are not persuasive. Caselaw makes it abundantly clear that these conclusory allegations do not form the basis for ineffective assistance. "Conclusory allegations, standing alone will never support a finding that an attorney's performance was deficient. Smith v. State, 955 P.2d 734, 738 (Okl. Cr. 1998); see also, Perry v. State, 853 P.2d 198, 203 (Okl. Cr. 1993) (generalized claim of ineffectiveness for failing to file motions insufficient to meet burden under Strickland); Trice v. State, 912 P.2d 349, 355 n.24 (Okl.Cr. 1996) ("bare allegations of defense counsel's unpreparedness do not support a claim of ineffective assistance of counsel"); Boyd v. State, 839 P.2d 1363, 1373 (Okl. Cr. 1992) (generalized claim of inadequate investigation and preparation and failure to file unspecified motions insufficient to establish claim of ineffective assistance).

Petitioner's appellate counsel also meets the Strickland test of reasonably effective assistance of counsel. Appellate counsel carefully selected legal issues to be raised on appeal, and the Appeals Court denied relief. Petitioner alleges that his appellate counsel: (1) constructively denied him the right to counsel under Cronic, (2) did not provide the brief to Petitioner before filing, and (3) failed to cite the record, resulting in claims being barred.

Petitioner cannot succeed on his first complaint that he was appointed 4 different appellate counsel. While he did have multiple public defenders who represented him, a brief was filed in the appeal, and mere turnover or replacement of counsel does not amount to ineffective assistance of counsel.

Second, a failure of appellate counsel to provide the brief to the Petitioner before filing does not constitute a deficient performance of appellate counsel in violation of the Petitioner's Constitutional rights.

Third, Petitioner claims appellate counsel's failure to correctly cite all alleged instances of prosecutorial misconduct in the closing argument rises to the level of a constitutionally deficient representation. But Petitioner's allegations are not supported by the facts or the law.

Petitioner's claims of factual innocence are without merit, and his claims are best styled as evidence he would've liked to have admitted during his trial. It is improper for this Court to reconsider Petitioner's factual innocence with evidence not presented during trial and not preserved in the record. A review of the exhibits and testimony Petitioner seeks to introduce shows that it's neither new evidence, as it was known of at the time of trial, nor inadmissible as it is not relevant to the allegations in the case. This Court must defer to the judgment the jury arrived at after deliberation with all the evidence before them, and consideration of the jury instructions, including the credibility of witnesses.

Petitioner also alleges that he was denied due process of law because of the evidence deemed inadmissible in his trial and his trial counsel's performance. These issues were raised on direct appeal and affirmed by the Court of Criminal Appeals and shown to be wholly without merit. Additionally, Petitioner has failed to provide


this Court with any showing of prejudice beyond mere conclusory statements. Due process claims have previously been adjudicated, and this Court will not disturb them. Aside from the ineffective assistance of appellate counsel claim, the Petitioner's claims are barred by the doctrine of waiver and res judicata. Petitioner admits in his brief that these claims are waived but attempts to overcome waiver and res judicata with an ineffective assistance of appellate counsel argument. However, appellate counsel raised these arguments in the appeal that was denied by the Court of Criminal Appeals. Thus, consideration of his ineffective assistance of appellate counsel claim is procedurally barred and is also without merit.

IT IS THEREFORE ORDERED, ADJUDGED, AND DECREED THAT the Petitioner's Application for Post-Conviction Relief is hereby Denied.
It is so ordered this 13th day of February 2026.

  STATEMENT OF THE CASE
Petitioner Dallas Christopher Norton was convicted by jury on June 24, 2022, of two counts of Lewd Acts with a Child Under 16 (21 O.S. § 1123) in Cleveland County District Court Case No. CF-2019-1273. The jury assessed punishment at twenty-five years imprisonment on each count. On August 31, 2022, the Honorable Thad Balkman pronounced judgment and sentence, ordering the sentences served consecutively, suspending all but eighteen years of the sentence in Count 1 and the entire twenty-five-year sentence in Count 2. Petitioner timely filed a direct appeal, iCase No. F-2022-746, raising five propositions of error. Appellate counsel Virginia Sanders filed a brief on April 12, 2023. On July 3, 2024, the Oklahoma Court of Criminal Appeals issued a Summary Opinion affirming the convictions. On June 30, 2025, Petitioner, proceeding pro se, filed an Application for Post-Conviction Relief. On July 24, 2025, the District Court issued an order to conform with district court rules regarding page limitations. PROPOSITION I

Standard of Review
This Court reviews the denial of post-conviction relief for an abuse of discretion, a standard met when a ruling is clearly erroneous or contrary to law. Boyd v. State, 1996 OK CR 12, 915 P.2d 922, 924. Where, as here, the district court misapplies procedural bars and ignores statutory exceptions, reversal is mandatory. See Logan v. State, 2013 OK CR 2, 293 P.3d 969, 973.
Argument
The district court’s order is a legal and factual fiction. Its entire basis for denying relief—that “each of Petitioner’s arguments were, or could have been, raised on direct appeal” and were in fact “raised” (Order, pp. 2, 8)—is an invention, flatly contradicted by the record. The direct appeal was a narrow, anemic affair that completely failed to challenge the core constitutional violations that corrupted Petitioner’s conviction. Appellate counsel’s failure to raise meritorious, outcome-determinative claims is the precise “sufficient reason” that dismantles procedural bars under 22 O.S. § 1086.
   This is not waiver; it is a constitutional breakdown. See Logan, 293 P.3d at 973. The district court ignored this command, inventing a procedural roadblock where the Legislature and this Court built a bypass for injustice.
   The direct appeal merely nibbled at the edges of the case, presenting only a handful of superficially, inadequately argued issues. In stark contrast, the post-conviction application unearthed the rot at its core, presenting for the first time a slate of “Clearly Stronger” and “Plainly Meritorious” “dead-bang winners” that appellate counsel completely abandoned. These are not re-packaged arguments; they are the arguments that would have reversed the conviction on direct appeal, and they are properly before this Court now because of appellate counsel’s total failure. The district court’s application of res judicata is, therefore, a profound legal error built on a lie.

Appellate counsel’s performance was not merely deficient; it was a wholesale abdication of duty that left a plethora of egregious constitutional violations unchallenged. Legal Insufficiency Under Applegate
 (The Uncorroborated, Impeached Testimony): The direct appeal’s generic sufficiency argument was a feckless gesture that ignored the dispositive legal issue. The correct, case-ending argument was that the State’s entire case rested on the uncorroborated testimony of two witnesses—the complainant and her father—whom the trial court itself deemed so unreliable that it issued OUJI-CR 9-20 impeachment instructions for both (Ex. K6, K7). Under Oklahoma’s clear and controlling precedent, when a complaining witness’s testimony is so thoroughly impeached as to be unworthy of belief, it legally requires corroboration to sustain a conviction. Applegate v. State, 904 P.2d 130 (Okla. Crim. App. 1995). The State presented zero corroborating evidence—no physical evidence, no medical evidence, no forensic evidence, and no credible outcry witness. The conviction was therefore legally insufficient as a matter of law. This was not a jury question; it was a legal bar to conviction that appellate counsel utterly failed to raise.

THE DISTRICT COURT ERRED BY APPLYING A “NEWLY DISCOVERED” STANDARD TO PETITIONER’S FACTUAL INNOCENCE CLAIM IGNORING STATE AND FEDERAL LAW
Standard of Review
A district court abuses its discretion when it applies an incorrect legal standard. Logan v. State, 2013 OK CR 2, ¶ 5, 293 P.3d 969, 973. The interpretation of 22 O.S. § 1080(D) and the governing actual-innocence standard are questions of law reviewed de novo. When a state court’s interpretation of its own statute imposes a barrier that directly contradicts governing Supreme Court precedent, this Court must follow the federal rule.McQuiggin v. Perkins, 569 U.S. 383, 397–98 (2013). See Fontenot v. Crow, 4 F.4th 982, 1030–34 (10th Cir. 2021).
Argument
The district court denied Petitioner's factual-innocence claim in a single paragraph, ruling that the evidence was "not new" because it "was known of at the time of trial" and was "not relevant." (Order, p. 6). In doing so, the court applied a diligence requirement borrowed from the new-trial statute, 22 O.S. § 952—effectively requiring Petitioner to show the evidence "could not have been produced at trial with reasonable diligence"—rather than the standard prescribed by 22 O.S. § 1080(D). This was an abuse of discretion. The correct standard under § 1080(D), U.S. Supreme Court and other controlling federal authority is whether the evidence is "not previously presented and heard" see, Fontenot, Schlup, Perkins, supra—not whether it could have been discovered earlier. Because Petitioner's documentary proof of physical impossibility (Exhibits J1–J8) was never presented to the jury and was only developed through post-conviction investigation, it falls squarely within the statutory definition. The court's refusal to consider it on the merits, based on an incorrect legal test, requires reversal.
A. The Plain Text of 22 O.S. § 1080(D) Contains No Diligence Requirement: Section 1080(D) provides relief based on "evidence of material facts, not previously presented and heard, that requires vacation of the conviction or sentence in the interest of justice." The Legislature deliberately chose the phrase "not previously presented and heard"—not "not previously discoverable" or "could not have been produced with reasonable diligence."
By contrast, when the Legislature intended to impose a diligence requirement, it did so explicitly. The new-trial statute, 22 O.S. § 952, requires that newly discovered evidence "could not have been produced at trial with reasonable diligence." The omission of that language from § 1080(D) is dispositive.
   Oklahoma courts have long held that statutes must be interpreted according to their plain language, and courts cannot add requirements the Legislature omitted. As the Oklahoma Court of Criminal Appeals has repeatedly stated:
"A rule of strict construction is to be applied to criminal statutes and courts should not extend them to embrace acts or conduct not clearly included within the prohibition of the statute." Simpson v. State, 1954 OK CR 28, 267 P.2d 1008, 1013.
"We must respect the authority and prerogatives of the Oklahoma Legislature, regardless of whether we agree with their actions." Dickens v. State, 2005 OK CR 4, 106 P.3d 599 (Okla. Crim. App. 2005).
"Words not found in the text of a criminal statute should not be read into it for the purpose of extending it or giving it an interpretation in conformity with a supposed policy." Quillen v. State, 2007 OK CR 22, 163 P.3d 587, 600 (Lumpkin, P.J., concurring in part/dissenting in part).
The Legislature knows how to impose a diligence requirement. It did so in § 952. It did not do so in § 1080(D). This Court cannot rewrite the statute.
The district court thus erred as a matter of law by engrafting § 952's diligence element onto § 1080(D). The correct inquiry was whether the evidence had been "presented and heard" at trial. It had not.
B. The District Court's Error Is Laid Bare by Comparing Its Ruling to the Actual Record.: The district court's order dismissed Petitioner's evidence with the following false findings. A direct comparison demonstrates the court's abuse of discretion:
What the District Court Said The Actual Standard and Record
Evidence is "not new" because it "was known of at the time of trial." (Order, p. 6) 
The statutory standard is "not previously presented and heard." 22 O.S. § 1080(D). The previous testimony from the record transcripts was not presented. The transcript of the protective order hearing, which was also not part of the case record, was not presented, Petitioner's lease documents, county assessor records, text messages, and photographs were never presented to the jury. They are "newly presented" as a matter of law.
"It is improper for this Court to reconsider Petitioner's factual innocence with evidence not presented during trial." (Order, p. 6) 
It’s not improper, it is the law. The Legislature expressly authorized reconsideration of factual innocence with "evidence not previously presented and heard." 22 O.S. § 1080(D). The district court's statement directly contradicts the statute and Federal Law. 
Evidence is "not relevant to the allegations in the case." (Order, p. 6)
The evidence proves a physical and temporal impossibility for the alleged crime. It is the most relevant evidence imaginable—proof that the crime could not have occurred.
"This Court must defer to the judgment the jury arrived at." (Order, p. 6)
This statement ignores that the jury never saw the dispositive evidence. Deference is owed to facts presented to the jury, not to a conviction based on an incomplete record.
   Each of the district court's findings is legally erroneous. The court applied the wrong standard, made factual findings contradicted by the record, and then used those findings to deny relief without ever reaching the merits.
C. Controlling Federal Authority Mandates a "Newly Presented" Standard for Actual-Innocence Claims: The actual-innocence gateway, recognized in Schlup v. Delo, 513 U.S. 298 (1995), and applied in Oklahoma post-conviction proceedings, uses a "newly presented" standard. Every federal circuit to address the question has held that the evidence need not be "newly discovered" in the sense of being previously unavailable; it is sufficient that it was "not presented at trial."
 Riva v. Ficco, 803 F.3d 77, 84 (1st Cir. 2015); Rivas v. Fischer, 687 F.3d 514, 543 (2d Cir. 2012); Hubbard v. Pinchak, 378 F.3d 333, 340 (3d Cir. 2004); Wolfe v. Dotson, 144 F.4th 218 (4th Cir. 2025); Cleveland v. Bradshaw, 693 F.3d 626, 633 (6th Cir. 2012); Gomez v. Jaimet, 350 F.3d 673, 679–80 (7th Cir. 2003); Griffin v. Johnson, 350 F.3d 956, 962–64 (9th Cir. 2003); Fontenot v. Crow, 4 F.4th 982, 1032 (10th Cir. 2021).
The Tenth Circuit—the court that will review any federal habeas petition arising from this conviction—has explicitly adopted the "newly presented" rule. Fontenot v. Crow, 4 F.4th 982, 1032 (10th Cir. 2021) ("We later clarified that 'new evidence' for an actual innocence claim means newly presented evidence, not newly discovered. Thus, a lack of diligence is not fatal to an actual innocence claim."). 
The Supreme Court in McQuiggin v. Perkins, 569 U.S. 383 (2013),  held that there is no categorical diligence barrier for actual-innocence gateway claims. Unlike procedural defaults that require cause and prejudice (where diligence matters), actual innocence itself serves as an exception—so the absence of diligence does not automatically bar relief.
"It would be bizarre to hold that a habeas petitioner who asserts a convincing claim of actual innocence may overcome [a statutory barrier], yet simultaneously encounter a court-fashioned diligence barrier to pursuit of her petition." Id. at 399.
Delay is considered only as a factor bearing on the credibility of the new evidence—not as a complete bar. Id.
Schlup itself repeatedly framed the inquiry as "new reliable evidence that was not presented at trial," Schlup, 513 U.S. at 324 (emphasis added), Schlup itself.turned on evidence that was previously available. Had the Court intended an unavailability or diligence requirement, "it would have said so." Id.
The actual-innocence gateway exists precisely because "in appropriate cases, the principles of comity and finality must yield to the imperative of correcting a fundamentally unjust incarceration." Id. at 320–21 (quoting Murray v. Carrier, 477 U.S. 478, 495 (1986)). As the Tenth Circuit recognized in Fontenot, the Schlup standard "does not demand conclusive proof of exoneration; rather, it involves a probabilistic determination that, in light of all the evidence—'old and new; admissible and inadmissible'—'more likely than not any reasonable juror would have reasonable doubt.'" 4 F.4th at 1030 (quoting House v. Bell, 547 U.S. 518, 538 (2006)). The district court's “previously available” diligence-based denial cannot be reconciled with governing precedent. When a state court’s interpretation of its own statute imposes a barrier that directly contradicts governing Supreme Court precedent, this Court must follow the federal rule. McQuiggin v. Perkins, 569 U.S. 383, 397–98 (2013). See Fontenot v. Crow, 4 F.4th 982, 1030–34 (10th Cir. 2021).
D. The District Court's Error Was Not Harmless.
Had the district court applied the correct "newly presented" standard, it would have been required to evaluate whether the newly presented evidence—considered alongside the trial record—established that "it is more likely than not that no reasonable juror would have found petitioner guilty beyond a reasonable doubt." Schlup, 513 U.S. at 327.
Petitioner's newly presented evidence (Exhibits J1–J8) includes lease documents, county assessor records, text messages, photographs, and sworn testimony proving a physical and temporal impossibility for the alleged crime. This evidence was never presented to the jury. The district court dismissed it with the single assertion that it was "not new" and "not relevant," ignoring the statutory standard and the controlling federal law that required its consideration.
Because the court applied the wrong legal test, its denial of the factual-innocence claim cannot stand.
Conclusion
The district court abused its discretion by applying a "newly discovered" diligence requirement to Petitioner's factual-innocence claim instead of the "newly presented" standard mandated by 22 O.S. § 1080(D) and controlling federal authority. The court's error was not harmless; it foreclosed any meaningful consideration of the dispositive evidence proving physical impossibility. This Court must reverse the denial of post-conviction relief and either vacate the conviction or remand for proper evaluation under the correct standard..
   PROPOSITION VII

Standard of Review
Compliance with the mandatory procedural rules of this Court is a question of law reviewed de novo. The failure to issue findings of fact and conclusions of law is a structural error frustrating all appellate review and requiring reversal. Cole v. State, 2007 OK CR 25, 164 P.3d 1089, 1091.

The district court’s order contains zero original findings of fact and zero original conclusions of law. It is a mindless copy of the State’s arguments. ORDER AFFIRMING DENIAL OF APPLICATION FOR POST-CONVICTION RELIEF

The Petitioner has appealed, pro se, to this Court from an order of the District Court of Cleveland County denying his application for post-conviction relief in Case No. CF-2019-1273. Petitioner was tried by a jury and found guilty of two counts of lewd or indecent acts with a child under sixteen. 

The jury assessed punishment at twenty-five years imprisonment in each count. The Honorable Thad Balkman, District Judge, pronounced judgment and ordered the sentences served consecutively, suspending all but eighteen years of the sentence in Count 1 and the entire twenty-five year sentence in Count 2. 

This Court affirmed Petitioner’s judgment and sentence on direct appeal. Norton v. State, No. F-2022-746 (Okl.Cr. July 3, 2024)(not for publication) 

Petitioner filed an application for post-conviction relief in the district court, raising ineffective assistance of appellate counsel, factual innocence, and cumulative error. Judge Balkman denied post-conviction relief in an order filed on February 13, 2026. Petitioner now appeals.

We review the district court’s determination for an abuse of discretion. State ex rel. Smith v. Neuwirth, 2014 OK CR 16, ¥ 12, 337 P.3d 763, 766. An abuse of discretion is any unreasonable or arbitrary action taken without proper consideration of the facts and law pertaining to the matter at issue or a clearly erroneous conclusion and judgment, one that is clearly against the logic and effect of the facts presented. Neloms v. State, 2012 OK CR 7, § 35, 274 P.3d 161, 170. “In the interests of efficiency and finality, our judicial system employs various doctrines to ensure that issues are not endlessly re-litigated.” Smith v. State, 2013 OK CR 14, J 14, 306 P.3d 557, 564. 

All issues that were previously raised and ruled upon in direct appeal proceedings or previous post-conviction proceedings are barred as res judicata, and all issues that could have been raised in those previous proceedings but were not, are waived, and may not be the basis of a subsequent post-conviction application. King v. State, 2001 OK CR 22, § 4, 29 P.3d 1089, 1090. 

Post-conviction review is not an opportunity for a second chance to argue claims of error in hopes that doing so in a different proceeding may change the outcome. Turrentine v. State, 1998 OK CR 44, { 12, 965 P.2d 985, 989. “Simply envisioning a new method of presenting an argument previously raised does not avoid the procedural bar.” McCarty v. State, 1999 OK CR 24, 9 9, 989 P.2d 990,995. “Appellate jurisprudence was not created or designed to allow a person convicted of a crime to continually challenge a conviction with new assertions of error.” Mayes v. State, 1996 OK CR 28, § 14, n.3, 921 P.2d 367, 372, n.3.

Many of the claims raised in Petitioner’s application for post-conviction relief involve issues that either were or could have been
raised in his direct appeal and are thus barred by res judicata or waived. 22 O.S.Supp.2022, § 1086; King, 2001 OK CR 29, § 4, 29 P.3d at 1090. He has not established any sufficient reason for not asserting or inadequately raising these grounds for relief in his direct appeal. Id.

Petitioner’s claim of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel is appropriate for review in a post-conviction application because this is the first opportunity for such review. See Logan v. State, 2013 OK CR 2, 4 5, 293 P.2d 969, 973. As set forth in Logan, post-conviction claims of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel are reviewed under the two-prong standard set forth in Strickland v. Washington, 466 U.S. 668 (1984). Id. To prevail on his ineffectiveness assistance claim, Petitioner must show that appellant counsel’s performance was deficient and that it prejudiced his defense. Strickland v. Washington, 466 U.S. 668, 687 (1984). Having reviewed Petitioner’s complaints under the Strickland analysis, we conclude that Petitioner has not rebutted the strong presumption that appellant counsel’s choices were the product of a reasonable strategy consistent with prevailing professional norms. Nor has Petitioner shown a reasonable probability that, but for these alleged errors, the result would have been different. The challenged acts and omissions do not satisfy either prong of the Strickland test.

Petitioner claims he is factually innocent. 
This Court’s rules and cases do not procedurally bar the raising of factual innocence claims in a post-conviction application. Slaughter v. State, 2005 OK CR6, 46, 108 P.3d 1052, 1054. “We fully recognize innocence claims are the Post-Conviction Procedure Act’s foundation.” Id. Claims of actual innocence may be raised for the first time on post-conviction, as it is possible that it might be a petitioner’s first opportunity to
allege and argue the issue. Braun v. State, 1997 OK CR 26, § 28 n.15, 937 P.2d 505, 514 n.15. 

The “actual innocence” exception applies only to factual innocence, where a petitioner can make a colorable showing he is actually innocent of the crime for which he is convicted. Id. 
Petitioner’s bald assertions fail to meet this standard. Petitioner does not cite any controlling authority supporting relief, and his claims are wholly unsupported by the record before this Court.

Petitioner fails to comprehend the nature of the burden a post-convictioner bears in making post-conviction claims before a trial court. 
“It is fundamental that where a post-conviction appeal is filed, the burden is upon the petitioner to sustain the allegations of his petition, and that every presumption favors the regularity of the proceedings had in the trial court. Error must affirmatively appear and is never presumed.” Russel v. Cherokee County Dist. Ct., 1968 OK CR 45, 9 5, 438 P.2d 293, 294. 

Having thoroughly reviewed Petitioner’s arguments and the record on appeal, Petitioner has not shown that the district court’s decision was clearly erroneous or clearly against the logic and effect of the facts presented.

Petitioner has failed to establish that he is entitled to post-conviction relief, the order of the District Court of Cleveland County in Case No. CF-2019-1273, denying Petitioner’s application for post-conviction relief is AFFIRMED. Pursuant to Rule 3.15, Rules of the Oklahoma Court of Criminal Appeals, Title 22, Ch.18, App. (2026), the MANDATE is ORDERED issued upon the delivery and filing of this decision.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

**FACTUAL BACKGROUND AND STATEMENT OF ACTUAL INNOCENCE**  
The State’s case against the Petitioner rests not on credible evidence, but on a narrative that systematically evolved to overcome exculpatory facts as they emerged. When this inconsistent testimony is measured against suppressed, objective evidence of the Petitioner’s physical location and the complainant’s own documented behavior, the conviction becomes a factual and physical impossibility. The complainant’s account transformed dramatically with each official interview, suggesting a fabricated story rather than the recall of a traumatic event. The initial police report from July 14, 2019, was fundamentally exonerating: the incident allegedly occurred on the living room couch, involved no completed act, and was confined to November–December 2018. Just eight days later, in a forensic interview, the narrative changed entirely. The location moved to the master bedroom, the act escalated to a forced touching, and yet, digital penetration was explicitly denied. By the preliminary hearing nearly six months later, the allegation of digital penetration appeared for the first time, directly contradicting her prior statement.

Throughout these shifting accounts, the one consistent element was the late-2018 timeframe, which the complainant described as “in the middle of fifth grade” and “before last Christmas.” At trial, the State attempted to create ambiguity by focusing on the phrase “before they got married.” However, this theory was demolished on cross-examination when the complainant herself clarified her own understanding, equating the late-2018 separation period with the phrase “before they got married.” The State ignored this dispositive testimony and falsely argued in closing that “Nobody ever said 2018.” This timeline is critical because objective, documentary evidence proves the Petitioner was not present during that period. The Complainant’s Testimony Was So Thoroughly Impeached as to Be Unworthy of Belief, Requiring Corroboration. Under Oklahoma law, a critical exception to the general rule on uncorroborated testimony is, where that testimony is not lucid, clear, and unambiguous it requires corroboration to sustain a conviction. The court's issuance of OUJI-CR 9-20 over the state's arguments (Ex- k6 K7) advised the jury of inconsistent statements existing called “Impeachment evidence” while this is a credibility instruction for the jury to determine believability, it still serves to show the testimony was in fact NOT lucid, clear, and unambiguous which a century of Oklahoma jurisprudence has held requires corroboration. This exception, established in cases from Salyer v. State, 1988 OK CR 246, 8, 761 P.2d 890, 893, to Jones v. State, 1988 OK CR 281, 10, 765 P.2d 800, 802, mandates that testimony requires corroboration when it "has been so thoroughly impeached as to be unworthy of belief." See also Camron v. The complainant’s testimony in this case falls squarely within this exception.

I. Petitioner’s Claims are barred by Res Judicata and Waiver


The Post-Conviction Procedure Act, Title 22 O.S. §1080, et seq., is neither a substitute for a direct appeal nor a means for a second appeal. Maines v. State, 1979 OK CR 71, 597 P.2d 774, 775-76; Fox v. State, 1994 OK CR 52, 880 P.2d 383, 384. The scope of this remedial measure is strictly limited and does not allow for litigation of issues available for review at the time of direct appeal. Castro v. State, 1994 OK CR 53, 880 P.2d 387, 388. Issues that were previously raised and ruled upon are procedurally barred from further review under the doctrine of res judicata; issues that were not raised previously on direct appeal, but which could have been raised, are waived for further review. Logan v. State, 2013 OK CR 2, ¶ 3, 293 P.3d 969, 973 (citing 22 O.S. 2001, § 1086; King v. State, 2001 OK CR 22, ¶ 4, 29 P.3d 1089, 1099; Webb v. State, 1992 OK CR 38, ¶ 6, 835 P.2d 115, 116, overruled on other grounds; Neill v. State, 1997 OK CR 41, ¶ 7 n.2, 943 P.2d 145, 148 n.2). “Post-conviction review was neither designed nor intended to provide applicants another direct appeal.” Id.; Cf. Coddington v. State, 2011 OK CR 21, ¶ 2, 259 P.3d 833, 835 (“The post-conviction process is not a second appeal.”).


An exception to this rule exists where a court finds sufficient reason for not asserting or inadequately presenting an issue in prior proceedings or “when an intervening change in constitutional law impacts the judgment and sentence.” Bryson v. State, 1995 OK CR 57, 903 P.2d 333, 334; 22 O.S. 2001 § 1086. Sufficient reason for failing to previously raise or adequately assert an issue requires a showing that some impediment external to the defense prevented the petitioner and counsel from properly raising the claim. Johnson v. State, 1991 OK CR 124, 823 P.2d 370, 373. “Petitioner has the burden of establishing that his alleged claim could not have been previously raised and thus is not procedurally barred." Robinson v. State, 1997 OK CR 24, 937 P.2d 101, 108.


In the present case, each of Petitioner's arguments were, or could have been, raised on direct appeal excluding his claim of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel. Petitioner does not offer this Court any reason, much less one external to the defense, for failing to previously assert these issues. Rather, he acknowledges that they have been waived and blames appellate counsel for not raising it, seeming to really mean that they didn't raise it the way he wanted them to, which does not override the waiver or res judicata implications of reraising these issues in post-conviction as a second appeal. Thus, consideration of these propositions is barred by the doctrine of res judicata and waiver, respectively. The Court of Criminal Appeals has stated that where a claim is procedurally barred, there is no need to address the merits of the issues presented. Boyd v. State, 1996 OK CR 12, 915 P.2d 922, 924. As aptly stated by the Court:


In the case sub judice, Petitioner was afforded an opportunity to pursue a direct appeal; he specifically declined to do so. As a result, he is bound by that earlier decision; as a consequence of that decision, he has forfeited his right to have this Court consider [issues], which would have been readily available for that direct appeal.


Wallace v. State, 1997 OK CR 18, 935 P.2d 366, 370 (citation omitted). Therefore, the allegations of error raised by Petitioner (specifically ineffective assistance of trial counsel, new evidence, and lack of due process) need not be addressed and Petitioner's Application for Post-Conviction Relief should be denied as a matter of law. Nonetheless, Petitioner's claims are wholly without merit.


II. Petitioner’s Ineffective Assistance of Trial and Appellate Counsel Claims Are Without Merit


Petitioner claims that he was constructively denied appellate counsel under United States v. Cronic. That case contemplates assistance of counsel at the trial stage, not the appellate stage,


since the adversarial process is not the same at the appellate stage as the trial stage. Appellate counsel filed a brief and the State responded to that brief and the Court of Criminal Appeals ruled on it. The appeal process was complete at that point, meaning no actual breakdown of the adversarial process. The proper standard to measure assistance of counsel is "reasonably effective assistance". In Strickland v. Washington, 466 U.S. 668, 104 S.Ct. 2052, 80 L.Ed.2d 674 (1984), the Court set out the proper standards for measuring effective assistance of counsel in a two-tier test. First, Petitioner is required to show that counsel's performance was deficient under prevailing professional norms, and that but for the deficient performance the outcome of his case would be different. This requires a showing that counsel made errors so serious that counsel was not functioning as the counsel guaranteed a defendant under the Sixth Amendment. Second, Petitioner must show that the deficient performance prejudiced his defense. This requires showing that counsel's errors were so serious as to deprive a defendant of a fair trial, a trial whose result is reliable. See also Workman v. State, 824 P. 2d, 378 (Okla.Cr. 1991). Furthermore, a defendant must overcome the presumption that, under the circumstances, the challenged action might be considered sound strategy because this court will indulge a strong presumption that counsel's conduct falls within the wide range of reasonable professional assistance." Strickland, 466 U.S. at 689, 104 S.Ct. at 2065.


Examination of the record reveals that Petitioner's arguments fail to overcome the first tier of the Strickland test. Petitioner's trial counsel acted as a reasonable competent attorney under the facts and circumstances of this case. The witnesses were cross-examined. Trial counsel submitted evidence during that cross-examination. Trial counsel objected to questioning or evidence that was potentially injurious to the client. A reviewing court should indulge "a strong presumption that counsel's conduct falls within the wide range of reasonably professional assistance", Strickland.




Moreover, the Court of Criminal Appeals found that the trial counsel’s performance was reasonably competent.


Additionally, Petitioner alleges that trial counsel failed to adequately prepare, have a trial strategy, and call sufficient witnesses. “[W]hen a defendant chooses to have a lawyer manage and present his case, law and tradition may allocate to counsel the power to make binding decisions of trial strategy in many areas.” Faretta v. California, 422 U.S. 806, 820, 95 S.Ct. 2523, 2534, 45 L.Ed. 2d 562 (1975). Included in counsel’s power to make decisions of strategy is the manner and scope of pretrial investigation. Counsel cannot be deemed ineffective for failing to investigate every possible avenue of defense. Browning v. State, 134 P.3d 816, 833 (Oklahoma.Cr. 2006). “An attorney who makes a strategic choice to channel his investigation into fewer than all plausible lines of defense upon which he bases his strategy are reasonable and his choices on the basis of those assumptions are reasonable.” Gilson v. State, 8 P.3d 883, 928 (Oklahoma.Cr. 2000) (quoting Boltz v. State, 806 P.2d 1117, 1126 (Oklahoma.Cr. 1991)). At trial, the determination of what witnesses will be presented, if any, on behalf of the defense is a matter of strategy left to counsel. DeLozier v. State, 991 P.2d 22, 31 (Oklahoma.Cr. 1998). There is a strong presumption that the decision not to present a witness or utilize available evidence is a reasonable trial strategy. Toles v. State, 947 P.2d 180, 188 (Oklahoma.Cr. 1997). So too is the decision to cross-examine witnesses and the manner in which it is done. Wilson v. State, 983 P.2d 448, 457 (Oklahoma.Cr. 1998). While Petitioner challenges the decisions of counsel in these areas of trial strategy, “[t]actical decisions, whether wise or unwise, successful or unsuccessful, cannot ordinarily form the basis for ineffective assistance.” Phillips v. State, 989 P.2d 1017, 1048 (Oklahoma.Cr. 1999).


Petitioner can make no showing of unreasonable performance by counsel and essentially reraises the same argument from the appellant brief he filed. As presented, Petitioner’s challenge


to the efficacy of counsel is nothing more than a conclusory allegation of deficient performance.


Yet, “[c]onclusory allegations, standing alone will never support a finding that an attorney’s performance was deficient. Smith v. State, 955 P.2d 734, 738 (Okla.Cr. 1998), see also, Perry v. State, 853 P.2d 198, 203 (Okla.Cr. 1993) (generalized claim of ineffectiveness for failing to file motions insufficient to meet burden under Strickland); Trice v. State, 912 P.2d 349, 355 n.24 (Okla.Cr. 1996) (“mere allegations of defense counsel’s unpreparedness do not support a claim of ineffective assistance of counsel”); Royal v. State, 839 P.2d 1363, 1373 (Okla.Cr. 1992) (generalized claim of inadequate investigation and preparation and failure to file unspecified motions insufficient to establish claim of ineffective assistance).


Appellate counsel is also held to the Strickland test of reasonably effective assistance. The Oklahoma Court of Criminal Appeals further defined the test in Cartwright v. State, 708 P.2d 592 (Okla. Cr. 1985). The Court held that counsel is not required to advance every argument, regardless of merit. See also Jones v. Barnes, 463 U.S. 745, 103 S.Ct. 3308, 77 L.Ed.2d 987 (1983), and Webb v. State, 835 P.2d 115 (Okla.Cr. 1992). In fact, appellate counsel’s refusal to raise nonfrivolous allegations may be evidence of effectiveness. Banks v. State, 810 P.2d 1286, 1290 (Okla.Cr. 1991), and Nguyen v. State, 844 P.2d 176, 178 (Okla.Cr. 1992). Thus, Petitioner’s argument that the “Numerous deficiencies effectively denied him of a fair and meaningful appeal” is without merit.


Petitioner’s appellate counsel carefully selected legal issues to be raised on appeal, and the Appeals Court denied relief. In Taylor v. State, 659 P.2d 362, 365 (Okla. Cr. 1983), the Court held “Lack of success is not the proper measure for determining the adequacy of legal representation”. Petitioner’s appellate counsel was reasonably competent. Petitioner’s allegation(s) of error fail to establish that the results of petitioner’s direct appeal would have been changed. Petitioner’s allegations equate to appellate counsel (1) constructively denying him the right to counsel under Cronic, (2) not providing the brief to Petitioner before filing, and (3) and failing to cite the record resulting in claims being barred.


First, Petitioner merely states that he was appointed 4 different appellate counsel who did things that together amounted to denial of counsel. However, for this claim to work, we have to (1) suspend the reasonable understanding that attorney have other clients they are working with - especially public defenders, (2) forget about the fact that a brief was filed in the appeal, (3) disregard the striking similarity of argument by Appellate Counsel (who we should believe is ineffective) and the Petitioner (acting pro se), and (4) punish the public defender's office for turnover in their office. None of this makes logical sense or amounts to ineffective assistance of counsel. Failing to provide a copy of the brief before filing does not constitute constitutionally deficient performance of appellate counsel. In fact, it is the purview of appellate counsel to carefully select claims to be presented to the Court to best ensure success. Additionally, claims raised by Petitioner in the present application shed light on the fact that claims Petitioner would have requested be included lacked merit.


Finally, Petitioner claims appellate counsel’s failure to correctly cite all alleged instances of prosecutorial misconduct in closing argument constituted ineffective assistance of appellate counsel. However, a mere procedural failure such as the one raised by the Petitioner is not sufficient to equate appellate counsel’s performance to that of constitutionally deficient representation. As a result, Petitioner’s allegations of ineffective assistance of both trial and appellate counsel must fail.

The Court further stated that the “actual innocence” exception is applicable only to factual innocence, where a petitioner can make a colorable showing he is actually innocent of the crime for which he is convicted; it is not applicable to legal innocence. Citations omitted.

* Correction: This grossly misrepresents the Judgment and Sentence. Petitioner was sentenced to 25 years on Count 1, with 18 years to be served in custody and 7 years suspended. On Count 2, he received a 25-year sentence, fully suspended, to run consecutively to Count 1. The State’s error confuses the terms and ignores the official record. There is no dispute of fact here; the State is simply incorrect.


Fabricated Motion for Suspended Sentence: The State claims: “On July 17, 2024, Petitioner filed a Motion for a Suspended Sentence which was denied by the Court” (State’s Resp., p. 2).
* Correction: No such motion exists in the official court docket or record. This statement is a complete fabrication. The State’s own footnote requests judicial notice of the docket (State’s Resp., p. 1 n.1), yet the docket confirms no such filing was ever made. This pattern of referencing “ghost documents” is deeply troubling. This is not a factual dispute; the State has invented a filing.


Misstatement of Direct Appeal Propositions: The State lists the direct appeal propositions but omits the fact that they were framed so narrowly as to be ineffective, failing to include key underlying issues like the victim’s impeachment, the use of false testimony in violation of Napue, or the powerful actual innocence claim (State’s Resp., p. 2).
* Correction: The direct appeal Brief (Case No. F-2022-746) argued insufficient evidence without citing the controlling authority or even mentioning the complainant was impeached, mandating reversal where a thoroughly impeached victim’s testimony lacks corroboration. It raised ineffective assistance of trial counsel on narrow grounds, omitting broader conflicts and failures. It challenged evidence admission but failed to raise the due process violation of the State’s knowing use of false testimony under Napue v. Illinois, 360 U.S. 264 (1959). The prosecutorial misconduct claim was limited to isolated instances, ignoring the pervasive vouching and other errors. No actual innocence claim was ever presented. The State does not dispute these omissions; it misrepresents the appeal’s scope to argue waiver, thereby admitting the facts of what was not raised.


Misstatement of Petitioner’s Claims: The State summarizes Petitioner’s claims but falsely implies they are mere rehashes of prior arguments, ignoring the central focus on IAAC as the gateway to review and the freestanding claim of actual innocence (State’s Resp., pp. 2-3).
* Correction: Petitioner’s claims are proper collateral attacks based on the deficiencies of the appellate process, not “second appeals.” The State admits the claims’ existence but mischaracterizes them legally, not factually.


B. Misstatements and Falsities in Proposition I (Res Judicata and Waiver)
False Claim of No “Sufficient Reason”: The State asserts Petitioner offers “no sufficient reason” external to the defense for not raising these issues previously, claiming he merely “blames appellate counsel” to circumvent procedural bars (State’s Resp., p. 4).
* Correction: This argument completely misapprehends Oklahoma post-conviction law. A meritorious claim of IAAC is the “sufficient reason” that overcomes waiver and res judicata. The Oklahoma Court of Criminal Appeals has repeatedly held that IAAC is a “paramount sufficient reason” to excuse what would otherwise be a procedural default. Logan v. State, 2013 OK CR 2, ¶5, 293 P.3d 969, 973. The State disputes no fact regarding appellate counsel’s omissions; it misinterprets the law, thereby admitting the factual basis of the IAAC claim.


Selective Omission of Authority: The State cites cases like Castro, Logan, and Coddington for the general rules of waiver but conveniently omits the explicit exceptions for IAAC that these very cases discuss (State’s Resp., pp. 3-4).
* Correction: As detailed in Petitioner’s Application, Castro v. State, 1994 OK CR 53, ¶3, 880 P.2d 387, 388, explicitly states that IAAC is an exception that lifts the procedural bars. Logan calls it “paramount.” The State misrepresents these holdings to fit its narrative, a legal error that concedes the factual basis for the IAAC claim.


False Characterization as “Second Appeal”: The State labels the Application a “second appeal” barred by res judicata (State’s Resp., p. 3).
* Correction: This characterization was rejected long ago. A post-conviction proceeding is a remedial action and a new cause of action, not a second appeal. Woodruff v. State, 1996 OK CR 5, ¶2, 910 P.2d 348, 350. The State raises no factual dispute, and its legal error admits the underlying facts.


C. Misstatements and Falsities in Proposition II (Ineffective Assistance Claims)
Misapplication of Cronic: The State falsely claims that the presumption of prejudice under United States v. Cronic, 466 U.S. 648 (1984), applies only at the trial stage, not the appellate stage (State’s Resp., p. 4).
* Correction: This is incorrect. The principles of Cronic, which find ineffective assistance per se where counsel entirely fails to subject the prosecution’s case to meaningful adversarial testing, extend to the appellate stage, particularly where counsel abandons meritorious claims. See Roe v. Flores-Ortega, 528 U.S. 470 (2000) (applying Strickland but discussing the role of counsel in preserving the appellate process itself). The State does not dispute the facts of counsel’s omissions (e.g., failure to raise BWC destruction, vouching); its legal misinterpretation admits them.


False “Sound Strategy” Presumption: The State reflexively presumes trial counsel’s actions were “sound strategy” (State’s Resp., pp. 5-6).
* Correction: Any presumption of strategy is overcome by the record. Counsel’s public betrayal of his client at sentencing and his admission of last-minute, from-memory preparation are not “strategy”—they are objective incompetence. The State cites general law (Strickland, Faretta) without disputing these specific, damning facts, thereby admitting them.


Misstatement of Appellate Counsel’s Role: The State claims appellate counsel need not raise every non-frivolous argument, citing Cartwright v. State, 1985 OK CR 4, 695 P.2d 548 (State’s Resp., p. 6).
* Correction: While true as a general principle, that rule does not apply where counsel omits “dead-bang winners”—clear, meritorious claims with a high probability of success. The State has raised no credible dispute regarding the strength of the omitted claims, and its legal misapplication admits the facts of those failures.


D. Misstatements and Falsities in Proposition III (Factual Innocence)
False Claim Evidence is “Not New”: The State asserts that the alibi and behavioral evidence was “known at trial” and is therefore not “new” and inadmissible now (State’s Resp., pp. 7-8).
* Correction: The State misunderstands the legal standard. Under 22 O.S. § 1080(4), a conviction can be vacated based on “evidence of material facts, not previously presented and heard.” The Tenth Circuit, interpreting federal habeas standards, has held that evidence of innocence, even if available at trial, can be considered “new” for an actual-innocence gateway claim if it was not presented to the jury. See Fontenot v. Crow, 4 F.4th 982 (10th Cir. 2021). The State does not dispute the facts of the physical impossibility (the consistent timeframe, the residency gap); its legal misinterpretation admits them.


Mischaracterization as “Retrying the Case”: The State claims Petitioner is merely attempting to “retry” the case with unavailable evidence (State’s Resp., p. 8).
* Correction: This is not a retrial; it is a collateral claim of actual innocence, which serves as a gateway to review otherwise barred constitutional claims, as established in Schlup v. Delo, 513 U.S. 298 (1995). The State’s lack of factual denial constitutes an admission.


E. Misstatements and Falsities in Proposition V (Appendix)
False Claim Appendix “Usurps” Court Order: The State alleges the 107-page Appendix improperly circumvents page limits (State’s Resp., p. 9).
* Correction: This is legally baseless. Rule 16(C) of the Rules of the Oklahoma Court of Criminal Appeals explicitly excludes appendices from page limitations. The 107-page appendix is necessary to document the egregious and pervasive campaign of prosecutorial misconduct in its entirety, establishing cumulative error under Pryor v. State, 2011 OK CR 18, 254 P.3d 721. The State’s failure to offer a factual denial of the overwhelming number of misconduct instances amounts to an admission.


F. Misstatements in Conclusion (Motions to Disqualify)

The U.S. Supreme Court has held that due process concerns “must override any statutory or procedural scheme” where there is a serious risk of actual bias. Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co., 556 U.S. 868, 886-87 (2009). ORDER AFFIRMING DENIAL OF APPLICATION FOR POST-CONVICTION RELIEF

The Petitioner has appealed, pro se, to this Court from an order of the District Court of Cleveland County denying his application for post-conviction relief in Case No. CF-2019-1273. Petitioner was tried by a jury and found guilty of two counts of lewd or indecent acts with a child under sixteen. 

The jury assessed punishment at twenty-five years imprisonment in each count. The Honorable Thad Balkman, District Judge, pronounced judgment and ordered the sentences served consecutively, suspending all but eighteen years of the sentence in Count 1 and the entire twenty-five year sentence in Count 2. 

This Court affirmed Petitioner’s judgment and sentence on direct appeal. Norton v. State, No. F-2022-746 (Okl.Cr. July 3, 2024)(not for publication) 

Petitioner filed an application for post-conviction relief in the district court, raising ineffective assistance of appellate counsel, factual innocence, and cumulative error. Judge Balkman denied post-conviction relief in an order filed on February 13, 2026. Petitioner now appeals.

We review the district court’s determination for an abuse of discretion. State ex rel. Smith v. Neuwirth, 2014 OK CR 16, ¥ 12, 337 P.3d 763, 766. An abuse of discretion is any unreasonable or arbitrary action taken without proper consideration of the facts and law pertaining to the matter at issue or a clearly erroneous conclusion and judgment, one that is clearly against the logic and effect of the facts presented. Neloms v. State, 2012 OK CR 7, § 35, 274 P.3d 161, 170. “In the interests of efficiency and finality, our judicial system employs various doctrines to ensure that issues are not endlessly re-litigated.” Smith v. State, 2013 OK CR 14, J 14, 306 P.3d 557, 564. 

All issues that were previously raised and ruled upon in direct appeal proceedings or previous post-conviction proceedings are barred as res judicata, and all issues that could have been raised in those previous proceedings but were not, are waived, and may not be the basis of a subsequent post-conviction application. King v. State, 2001 OK CR 22, § 4, 29 P.3d 1089, 1090. 

Post-conviction review is not an opportunity for a second chance to argue claims of error in hopes that doing so in a different proceeding may change the outcome. Turrentine v. State, 1998 OK CR 44, { 12, 965 P.2d 985, 989. “Simply envisioning a new method of presenting an argument previously raised does not avoid the procedural bar.” McCarty v. State, 1999 OK CR 24, 9 9, 989 P.2d 990,995. “Appellate jurisprudence was not created or designed to allow a person convicted of a crime to continually challenge a conviction with new assertions of error.” Mayes v. State, 1996 OK CR 28, § 14, n.3, 921 P.2d 367, 372, n.3.

Many of the claims raised in Petitioner’s application for post-conviction relief involve issues that either were or could have been
raised in his direct appeal and are thus barred by res judicata or waived. 22 O.S.Supp.2022, § 1086; King, 2001 OK CR 29, § 4, 29 P.3d at 1090. He has not established any sufficient reason for not asserting or inadequately raising these grounds for relief in his direct appeal. Id.

Petitioner’s claim of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel is appropriate for review in a post-conviction application because this is the first opportunity for such review. See Logan v. State, 2013 OK CR 2, 4 5, 293 P.2d 969, 973. As set forth in Logan, post-conviction claims of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel are reviewed under the two-prong standard set forth in Strickland v. Washington, 466 U.S. 668 (1984). Id. To prevail on his ineffectiveness assistance claim, Petitioner must show that appellant counsel’s performance was deficient and that it prejudiced his defense. Strickland v. Washington, 466 U.S. 668, 687 (1984). Having reviewed Petitioner’s complaints under the Strickland analysis, we conclude that Petitioner has not rebutted the strong presumption that appellant counsel’s choices were the product of a reasonable strategy consistent with prevailing professional norms. Nor has Petitioner shown a reasonable probability that, but for these alleged errors, the result would have been different. The challenged acts and omissions do not satisfy either prong of the Strickland test.

Petitioner claims he is factually innocent. 
This Court’s rules and cases do not procedurally bar the raising of factual innocence claims in a post-conviction application. Slaughter v. State, 2005 OK CR6, 46, 108 P.3d 1052, 1054. “We fully recognize innocence claims are the Post-Conviction Procedure Act’s foundation.” Id. Claims of actual innocence may be raised for the first time on post-conviction, as it is possible that it might be a petitioner’s first opportunity to
allege and argue the issue. Braun v. State, 1997 OK CR 26, § 28 n.15, 937 P.2d 505, 514 n.15. 

The “actual innocence” exception applies only to factual innocence, where a petitioner can make a colorable showing he is actually innocent of the crime for which he is convicted. Id. 
Petitioner’s bald assertions fail to meet this standard. Petitioner does not cite any controlling authority supporting relief, and his claims are wholly unsupported by the record before this Court.

Petitioner fails to comprehend the nature of the burden a post-convictioner bears in making post-conviction claims before a trial court. 
“It is fundamental that where a post-conviction appeal is filed, the burden is upon the petitioner to sustain the allegations of his petition, and that every presumption favors the regularity of the proceedings had in the trial court. Error must affirmatively appear and is never presumed.” Russel v. Cherokee County Dist. Ct., 1968 OK CR 45, 9 5, 438 P.2d 293, 294. 

Having thoroughly reviewed Petitioner’s arguments and the record on appeal, Petitioner has not shown that the district court’s decision was clearly erroneous or clearly against the logic and effect of the facts presented.

Petitioner has failed to establish that he is entitled to post-conviction relief, the order of the District Court of Cleveland County in Case No. CF-2019-1273, denying Petitioner’s application for post-conviction relief is AFFIRMED. Pursuant to Rule 3.15, Rules of the Oklahoma Court of Criminal Appeals, Title 22, Ch.18, App. (2026), the MANDATE is ORDERED issued upon the delivery and filing of this decision.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

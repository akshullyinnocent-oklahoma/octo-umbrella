GROUND 2: PETITIONER IS ACTUALLY INNOCENT - UNDER SCHLUP v. DELO

Issue: Whether Petitioner has satisfied the Schlup actual innocence gateway so that any procedural default of his other constitutional claims is excused to prevent a fundamental miscarriage of justice.

Rule: Under Schlup v. Delo, 513 U.S. 298 (1995), a petitioner who presents new, reliable evidence of actual innocence may overcome procedural bars if he shows that “it is more likely than not that no reasonable juror would have convicted him in light of the new evidence.” Id. at 327. The court must consider “all the evidence, old and new, admissible and inadmissible,” House v. Bell, 547 U.S. 518, 538 (2006), and make a “holistic judgment.” Fontenot v. Crow, 4 F.4th 982, 1031 (10th Cir. 2021). The gateway applies to claims of “factual innocence, not legal innocence.” Bousley v. United States, 523 U.S. 614, 623 (1998). It exists because “principles of comity and finality must yield to the imperative of correcting a fundamentally unjust incarceration.” Schlup, 513 U.S. at 320–21.

Facts: As detailed in the freestanding claim above and throughout this petition, Petitioner has presented substantial new evidence that was never presented to the jury. That evidence includes:

· The complainant’s consistent pretrial statements locking the event to late 2018 (Ex. A1-A3, B3, C3-C7, D4-D14).
· Documentary proof that Petitioner was absent from the home from September 8, 2018, until December 30, 2018 (Ex. J1-J6).
· Proof that the complainant was in Arizona from December 22, 2018, to January 5, 2019 (Ex. J7-J8).
· Real-time messages and photos showing the complainant’s continuing affectionate behavior toward Petitioner throughout the alleged timeframe (Ex. J12-J15, J19-J26).
· Emails confirming trial counsel possessed this evidence but never used it (Ex. J31-J34), and the record of his coerced waiver of Petitioner’s right to testify (Ex. L2-L3, I1-I3).

The trial record itself contains impeachment of the State’s two primary witnesses (Ex. K6-K7), false testimony from multiple witnesses, the prosecutor’s knowing false statement (Ex. H2), and the destruction of the BWC recording of the initial disclosure (Ex. A5, F17-F18, G3-G6).

Analysis: This new evidence, considered alongside the trial record, makes it more likely than not that no reasonable juror would convict. The complainant’s own repeated, unwavering timeline places the crime in a window when Petitioner could not have been present. The documentary evidence is objective and compelling. The behavioral evidence directly contradicts the allegation of abuse. When a jury, unaware of this evidence, was left with only the evolving, impeached, and contradictory testimony of the State’s witnesses, the verdict is fatally unreliable.

The new evidence satisfies Schlup’s requirement of reliability—it consists of contemporaneous documents, photographs, text messages, and sworn testimony. It was not presented at trial because of counsel’s ineffectiveness and coercion. Under Fontenot, evidence is “new” if not presented at trial, regardless of when it was discovered. 4 F.4th at 1032. The state courts’ failure to recognize this gateway, and their insistence on procedural bars without considering the actual innocence evidence, was contrary to and an unreasonable application of Schlup, House, and McQuiggin.

Conclusion: Petitioner has satisfied the actual innocence gateway under Schlup. All procedural bars to his claims—including ineffective assistance of trial counsel, Napue and Brady/Youngblood violations, prosecutorial misconduct, coerced waiver of the right to testify, and conflict of interest—are excused. This Court must reach the merits of those claims and, for the reasons set forth in this petition, grant the writ of habeas corpus. The continued incarceration of an innocent man is a fundamental miscarriage of justice that the Constitution will not tolerate.

GROUND 3: FAILURE TO PRESENT EXCULPATORY ALIBI EVIDENCE

Issue: Whether trial counsel rendered ineffective assistance by failing to present documentary evidence---in his possession and promised in opening statement---proving Petitioner was absent from the home during December 2018, the only timeframe the complainant ever alleged.

Rule: A defendant must show (1) counsel’s performance was deficient, and (2) the deficiency prejudiced the defense. Strickland v. Washington, 466 U.S. 668, 687 (1984). Counsel has a duty to investigate and present available exculpatory evidence, and a “cursory investigation” does not justify a tactical decision to omit it. Wiggins v. Smith, 539 U.S. 510, 523 (2003). Abandoning a verifiable alibi without strategic justification is deficient. Cargle v. Mullin, 317 F.3d 1196, 1202 (10th Cir. 2003). Failing to produce evidence promised in an opening statement undermines defense credibility and lets the jury infer the evidence would have been adverse. Conley v. State, 2014 Ark. 172, ¶ 35.

Facts: The complainant consistently placed the events in December 2018. Counsel possessed, but never introduced: a lease for a new residence signed September 4, 2018 (Ex. J1); a photograph of a truck and trailer at the new residence, September 8, 2018 (Ex. J3); county assessor records confirming the address (Ex. J2); texts from September 8, 2018: “I’m in the middle of moving” (Ex. J4–J5); messages documenting the complainant’s departure for Arizona on December 22, 2018 (Ex. J7) and return on January 5, 2019 (Ex. J8); and Facebook posts confirming Petitioner returned to the residence on December 30, 2018 (Ex. J6). State witnesses confirmed Petitioner “moved out Summer 18” (Ex. D6) and “never spent the night” during the separation (Ex. F13). In opening statement, counsel promised the jury Arizona child welfare and law enforcement testimony corroborating the alibi (Tr. II 21, 26–27). Counsel rested without calling a single defense witness or introducing any documents (Tr. III 165).

Analysis: The evidence proved physical impossibility: Petitioner and the complainant were not in the home together during December 2018. Counsel’s failure to present this cannot be excused as strategy. Where counsel was aware of alibi evidence but “raised no defense at all,” his representation was “totally ineffective.” The unfulfilled opening-statement promise compounded the prejudice; “[l]ittle is more damaging than to fail to produce important evidence that had been promised in an opening.” Conley, 2014 Ark. 172, at ¶ 35. Had the jury seen the lease, photographs, and text messages, no rational juror could have convicted.

Conclusion: Counsel’s failure to present available, conclusive alibi evidence deprived Petitioner of his only defense and his Sixth Amendment right to effective assistance. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 4: GROUND: FAILURE TO SUBPOENA AND CALL MATERIAL WITNESSES

Issue: Whether trial counsel rendered ineffective assistance by promising in opening statement that Shyanne Pike and Arizona DCS worker Heather Slater would testify, failing to subpoena Slater, and then resting without calling any witnesses and without any strategic justification.

Rule: The right to present a defense includes the right to call witnesses whose testimony is relevant and material. Washington v. Texas, 388 U.S. 14, 19 (1967). Promising evidence in opening statement and then failing to produce it without explanation is deficient and prejudicial. Anderson v. Butler, 858 F.2d 16, 17 (1st Cir. 1988) (“little is more damaging than to fail to produce important evidence that had been promised in an opening”); Conley v. State, 2014 Ark. 172, ¶ 35. Failure to call listed witnesses whose testimony supports the alibi theory is deficient. Cargle v. Mullin, 317 F.3d 1196, 1202 (10th Cir. 2003). Where reasons for abandonment were apparent at the time the promises were made, the failure cannot be described as legitimate trial strategy. Felts v. State, 354 S.W.3d 266, 277-78 (Tenn. 2011).

Facts: In opening statement, counsel told the jury: “You’ll hear from Shyanne Pike. She’ll describe how they went fishing. Brianna asked to go fishing with them. She took them to a farm that her family owns and did things with her out there” (Tr. II 21). Heather Slater, an Arizona DCS worker, was identified on the Defense witness list as someone who would testify consistent with her statements, reports, and documents (Ex. K10). Despite being listed, Slater was never subpoenaed (Docket-Main.txt). Neither Pike nor Slater was called. Counsel rested without presenting any witnesses (Tr. III 165). The record contains no strategic explanation. Pike would have testified that the complainant voluntarily spent time with Petitioner during the separation, contradicting the claim that she feared him. Slater would have provided official Arizona records corroborating the complainant’s statements there.

Analysis: Under Anderson, “little is more damaging than to fail to produce important evidence that had been promised in an opening.” 858 F.2d at 17. Under Conley, when counsel primes the jury to hear a different version of events, “one may infer that reasonable jurors would think the witnesses to which counsel referred in his opening statement were unwilling or unable to deliver the testimony he promised.” 2014 Ark. 172, ¶ 35. The unfulfilled promise destroyed counsel’s credibility and left the jury to infer the evidence would have been adverse. Under Felts, making promises and then abandoning them for reasons apparent at the time cannot be legitimate strategy. Counsel’s failure to subpoena or call these witnesses left the promised alibi defense a hollow shell.

Conclusion: Counsel’s abandonment of promised material witnesses without justification deprived Petitioner of his right to present a defense. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 13: FAILURE TO STRIKE BIASED JUROR M.K.

GROUND 14: COERCED WAIVER OF RIGHT TO TESTIFY

Issue: Whether Petitioner’s waiver of his fundamental right to testify was invalid because it was coerced by trial counsel during a lunch break on the last day of trial, secured through a rushed, whispered bench colloquy with the jury present, and accepted by the court without any independent inquiry into voluntariness.

Rule: A criminal defendant’s right to testify in his own defense is personal and fundamental. Rock v. Arkansas, 483 U.S. 44, 52 (1987). Like the waiver of other constitutional rights, the defendant’s waiver of his right to testify must be made knowingly and voluntarily. Johnson v. Zerbst, 304 U.S. 458, 464 (1938); Eltabech v. Hopkins, 997 F.2d 386, 387 (8th Cir. 1993). Where counsel nullifies the defendant’s right to testify over his protest, the trial court has a duty to conduct further inquiry to ensure the waiver was not wrongly suppressed. Crawley v. Commonwealth, 107 S.W.3d 197, 203 (Ky. 2003); United States v. Pennycooke, 65 F.3d 9, 12 (3d Cir. 1995).

Facts: On the final day of testimony, during the lunch break, trial counsel informed Petitioner that the defense would call no witnesses and Petitioner “did not need to testify.” Petitioner’s contemporaneous letter to the court stated this was “not of my choosing” and that he was instructed to “just agree with what was asked” (Ex. I1-I3). The waiver colloquy was conducted at a whispered bench conference with the jury present; counsel stated, “I’m going to speak as low as I can,” and the prosecutor interrupted, “Kind of getting a little loud.” The court administered the oath but asked no questions. All questioning was by co-counsel Charles Douglas---the same law office---using leading questions that Petitioner had been told to agree with. Petitioner stated he was under extreme stress and felt he had no choice but to relent, and that he never intended to give up his right to defend himself.

Analysis: The waiver colloquy was a constitutional nullity. Under Rock, the right to testify is fundamental and personal. Under Johnson and Eltabech, waiver must be knowing and voluntary---neither element was satisfied. A whispered bench conference, rushed because the jury was waiting, conducted by the attorney who had just ambushed his client, with no independent judicial inquiry, is the antithesis of a valid waiver. Under Pennycooke, where counsel nullifies the defendant’s right over his protest, “the trial court [should] inquire discreetly into the disagreement and ensure that constitutional rights are not suppressed wrongly.” 65 F.3d at 12. Under Crawley, when the court has reason to believe the waiver was not knowing or intelligent, it has a duty to conduct further inquiry. The court asked nothing. Petitioner was denied the opportunity to present his alibi, to explain the documentary impossibility evidence, and to deny the allegations in his own words. The prejudice is structural.

Conclusion: Petitioner’s coerced waiver of his right to testify, accepted without meaningful judicial inquiry, violated his rights under the Fifth, Sixth, and Fourteenth Amendments. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 15: CONFLICT OF INTEREST AND PUBLIC CONTRADICTION OF CLIENT AT SENTENCING

Issue: Whether trial counsel created an actual conflict of interest and abandoned his duty of loyalty by refusing to argue Petitioner’s new-trial claims because he did not agree with them, stating he had no objection to Petitioner addressing the court, and then, when Petitioner testified about the coerced waiver, interrupting to tell the court his client’s account was false and becoming a witness against him.

Rule: An actual conflict of interest arises when counsel’s representation is adversely affected by his own interests or duties to others. Cuyler v. Sullivan, 446 U.S. 335, 348 (1980). Where counsel mocks a client’s claims or attacks his credibility, he demonstrates a “fundamental violation of his duty of loyalty.” Cargle v. Mullin, 317 F.3d 1196 (10th Cir. 2003). Prejudice is presumed once an actual conflict is shown. Cuyler, 446 U.S. at 348-49. The trial court has a duty to inquire when a conflict is apparent. Wood v. Georgia, 450 U.S. 261, 272 (1981).

Facts: At the combined sentencing and new-trial hearing, trial counsel informed the court that he could not argue paragraphs 8 through 12 of the new-trial motion because “those are not assertions that I necessarily agree with” and “I don’t --- I don’t agree with them” (Sent. Tr. 8-9). He then stated, “if Mr. Norton wants to be heard on those … then I suppose I have no objection to him being heard on those.” When Petitioner began testifying about the coerced waiver, counsel immediately interrupted: “I have to as my duty, for candor of the Court, I can’t allow him to testify to something I know is false. I didn’t say those words ever.” After Petitioner finished, counsel again told the court “much of what he just testified to is not true” and characterized his own client’s account as factually false (Sent. Tr. 10-12). Counsel made no motion to withdraw and pursued no other professional means to resolve the conflict; instead he created the conflict, invited the testimony, and then attacked it.

Analysis: Counsel’s actions created an actual conflict of interest under Cuyler. By refusing to argue claims because he personally disagreed, counsel placed his own interests above his client’s. By then inviting Petitioner to speak and immediately branding him a liar, counsel became a witness against his own client on the critical issue of the coerced waiver, abandoning the adversarial role and aligning with the prosecution. Under Cargle, when counsel mocked a petitioner’s claims and exhibited “actual doubt and hostility toward his client’s case,” the Tenth Circuit held it was a “fundamental violation of his duty of loyalty.” 317 F.3d at 1202. Here, counsel’s identical conduct---branding Petitioner a liar at sentencing---was not strategy but hostility. Under Cuyler, prejudice is presumed: “a defendant who shows that a conflict of interest actually affected the adequacy of his representation need not demonstrate prejudice in order to obtain relief.” 446 U.S. at 349-50. The sentencing court was left with the indelible impression that even Petitioner’s own lawyer believed him to be a liar.

Conclusion: Counsel’s open-court attack on his client’s credibility created an actual conflict of interest and abandoned the duty of loyalty. The conviction and sentence must be vacated.

GROUND 16: CUMULATIVE ERROR / CONSTRUCTIVE DENIAL OF COUNSEL (CRONIC)

Issue: Whether the cumulative effect of trial counsel’s multiple failures---abandoning the alibi defense, failing to call witnesses, failing to object to misconduct and improper evidence, failing to impeach perjured witnesses, coercing the waiver of the right to testify, creating a conflict of interest by attacking his own client, and conceding on the record his lack of preparation---amounted to a constructive denial of counsel under United States v. Cronic, 466 U.S. 648 (1984).

Rule: When counsel “entirely fails to subject the prosecution’s case to meaningful adversarial testing,” prejudice is presumed without inquiry into actual performance. United States v. Cronic, 466 U.S. 648, 659 (1984). Even if no single error is sufficient, the cumulative effect of multiple deficiencies can render counsel’s performance constitutionally ineffective. Cargle v. Mullin, 317 F.3d 1196, 1206 (10th Cir. 2003). A defendant whose counsel is unable to provide effective representation is in “no better position than one who has no counsel at all.” Evitts v. Lucey, 469 U.S. 387, 396 (1985).

Facts: As detailed in the preceding grounds, trial counsel: failed to present documentary alibi evidence proving Petitioner was absent from the home during the only timeframe alleged; failed to subpoena and call material witnesses promised in opening statement; failed to impeach Detective Judy, Tiffany Norton, Bailey Ferguson, and Christi Cornett with readily available contradictory testimony and documents; failed to object to the State’s use of an unauthenticated substitute forensic interview disk; failed to seek any remedy for the destruction of exculpatory BWC evidence; failed to request impeachment instructions due to admitted lack of preparation until 1:00 a.m. on the final day; failed to object to hundreds of instances of GROUND in voir dire and closing; failed to strike a clearly biased juror; coerced Petitioner into waiving his right to testify and then participated in a constitutionally defective colloquy; and created an actual conflict of interest by publicly branding Petitioner a liar at sentencing and refusing to argue his new-trial claims. The defense rested without calling a single witness or introducing a single exhibit.

Analysis: Counsel’s performance across every phase of trial---pretrial, voir dire, evidentiary phase, witness examination, instruction conference, and post-trial---was so deficient that the prosecution’s case was never subjected to meaningful adversarial testing. Under Cronic, when counsel “entirely fails” to function, prejudice is presumed. Here, the cumulative failures were not isolated tactical choices; they were a systemic breakdown of representation. Counsel promised a defense in opening and then abandoned it entirely. He allowed the State’s false evidence to go unchallenged. He coerced his client into silence. He then attacked his client’s credibility in open court. No rational strategy can explain this pattern. Under Cargle, the cumulative effect of these deficiencies deprived Petitioner of a fair trial. This is a textbook case of constructive denial of counsel.

Conclusion: The cumulative effect of trial counsel’s deficiencies constructively denied Petitioner the assistance of counsel and deprived him of a fair trial. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 19: FAILURE TO CONDUCT PROPER WAIVER COLLOQUY

Issue: Whether the trial court violated Petitioner's constitutional rights by accepting his waiver of the right to testify without conducting any independent inquiry into voluntariness, relying solely on leading questions from the same counsel who had just coerced the waiver during a lunch break, in a rushed, whispered bench conference with the jury present.

Rule: A criminal defendant's right to testify in his own defense is personal and fundamental. Rock v. Arkansas, 483 U.S. 44, 52 (1987). Waiver of this right must be knowing, intelligent, and voluntary. Johnson v. Zerbst, 304 U.S. 458, 464 (1938). The Johnson v. Zerbst standard applies in determining whether the defendant has waived the right to testify. Hunter v. United States, 588 A.2d 680, 684 (D.C. 1991). Where counsel nullifies the defendant's right over his protest, the trial court has a duty to conduct further inquiry to ensure the waiver was not wrongly suppressed. Crawley v. Commonwealth, 107 S.W.3d 197, 203 (Ky. 2003); United States v. Pennycooke, 65 F.3d 9, 12 (3d Cir. 1995). The trial court has an independent duty to inquire when circumstances suggest the waiver may not be voluntary. Wood v. Georgia, 450 U.S. 261, 272 (1981).

Facts: The waiver colloquy was conducted at a whispered bench conference while the jury remained in the courtroom. Co-counsel Charles Douglas began: "I'm going to speak as low as I can" (Ex. L2). The prosecutor interrupted, "Kind of getting a little loud," and asked that Petitioner be repositioned (Ex. L2). The court administered the oath but asked no questions during the entire colloquy. All questioning was by Mr. Douglas, using leading questions such as: "Have you felt any pressure from Mr. Nedwick or myself in respect to either preparing the defense or calling witnesses?" and "And has Mr. Nedwick or myself at all ever pressured you into making the decision not to call witnesses?" and "Do you make this freely of your own free will and volition?" (Ex. L2-L6). The court never asked Petitioner whether the decision was his own, whether he understood his right, whether he had been pressured or threatened, or why a defense that had been planned for years was suddenly being abandoned mid-trial. At the conclusion, the court stated only: "Okay. I have no further questions. Thank you" (Ex. L7). Petitioner's contemporaneous letter stated the waiver was "not of my choosing" and that he was instructed to "just agree with what was asked" (Ex. I1-I3).

Analysis: Under Rock, the right to testify is fundamental and personal. Under Johnson and Hunter, waiver must be knowing and voluntary. A colloquy in which the judge asks nothing, and all inquiry comes from the attorney accused of coercion, is no colloquy at all. Under Pennycooke, where counsel nullifies the defendant's right over his protest, "the trial court [should] inquire discreetly into the disagreement and ensure that constitutional rights are not suppressed wrongly." 65 F.3d at 12. Under Crawley, when the court has reason to believe the waiver was not knowing or intelligent, it has a duty to conduct further inquiry. 107 S.W.3d at 203. Under Wood, when the possibility of a constitutional violation is apparent, the court must inquire further. Here, the circumstances themselves---a mid-trial, last-minute abandonment of a planned defense, a whispered bench conference, the prosecutor's concern about volume, co-counsel's leading questions---all placed the court on notice that further inquiry was required. The court asked nothing. Petitioner was denied the opportunity to present his alibi, to explain the documentary impossibility evidence, and to deny the allegations in his own words. The prejudice is structural.

Conclusion: The trial court's failure to conduct an independent waiver colloquy violated Petitioner's right to due process. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 20: FAILURE TO CORRECT PROSECUTOR'S FALSE STATEMENT

Issue: Whether the trial court violated due process by failing to correct the prosecutor's knowingly false statement during closing that "no one ever said 2018" despite the court's awareness of multiple record references to the December 2018 timeframe.

Rule: The prosecutor must "apprise the court when he knows that his witness is giving testimony that is substantially misleading... when it should be obvious to the Government that the witness' answer, although made in good faith, is untrue, the Government's obligation to correct that statement is as compelling." People v. Smith, 498 Mich. 466, 476, 870 N.W.2d 299, 305 (2015). If false testimony "affected or might have affected the judgment of the jury then a conviction must fall." Mills v. Scully, 653 F. Supp. 885, 895 (S.D.N.Y. 1987). The trial court has an inherent duty to ensure a fair trial; when the prosecutor makes a demonstrably false statement about the record that the court knows to be false, judicial silence constitutes a failure to ensure fundamental fairness.

Facts: During closing argument, the prosecutor told the jury: "Defense argues that it was December of '18 is what she's saying. No one ever said 2018. If she said November or December to her dad, it could have been the Christmas before" (Tr. IV 95-96; Ex. H2). This statement was demonstrably false. The police report listed the occurrence from November 29, 2018 through December 25, 2018 (Ex. A1-A2). The VPO testimony anchored events to "before last Christmas" and "before Christmas" (Ex. C3, C5-C6). The preliminary hearing testimony anchored events to "middle of fifth grade" and "medium time before Christmas" (Ex. D4, D14). The court had presided over the entire trial and was aware of this record evidence. The court remained silent, issued no curative instruction, and made no effort to correct the false statement. The jury retired to deliberate minutes later with the false assertion uncorrected.

Analysis: The prosecutor's false statement directly concealed the timeline impossibility from the jury---the defense's central theory. Under Smith, the prosecutor had a duty to correct this known falsehood; the court, having presided over the introduction of all contradictory evidence, was equally aware the statement was false. Its duty to ensure a fair trial required it to intervene. The court's silence in the face of this known prosecutorial violation constituted an abdication of its role as a neutral arbiter, leaving the jury with a false factual premise for its deliberations. Under Mills, the falsehood "might have affected the judgment of the jury," as it directly undermined the defense theory of physical impossibility. The timeline was the key to the defense; the uncorrected false statement foreclosed the jury from considering that the alleged crime could not have occurred as charged.

Conclusion: The trial court's failure to correct the prosecutor's false statement violated due process. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 21: FAILURE TO GIVE LIMITING INSTRUCTION FOR FERGUSON TESTIMONY

Issue: Whether the trial court violated due process by admitting Bailey Ferguson's highly prejudicial res gestae testimony without any limiting instruction to prevent the jury from using it as propensity evidence.

Rule: "[T]he importance of giving a limiting instruction, particularly when the other acts were not substantially simultaneous in time and circumstances, is no less than when the evidence is admitted under CRE 404(b)... trial courts should continue to follow 'the better practice' of giving a limiting instruction when requested to do so." People v. Agado, 964 P.2d 565, 567 (Colo. Ct. App. 1998) (Vogt, J., specially concurring). The admission of highly prejudicial other-acts evidence without any safeguard against propensity reasoning renders a trial fundamentally unfair and violates due process.

Facts: The court admitted Ferguson's testimony as res gestae at the January 31, 2022 hearing, ruling it was "central to the chain of events" (Ex. 1/31/22 Tr. 57). When defense counsel requested a limiting instruction, the prosecutor argued that "if it's res gestae, there is no limiting instruction" and that a limiting instruction "would define it as Burks, and you haven't defined it as Burks" (Ex. 1/31/22 Tr. 57-58). The court agreed, stating: "My intent is not to have a specific instruction... the testimony from Ms. Ferguson relating to the exchanges with Mr. Norton is res gestae evidence that would not qualify or necessitate a separate instruction, as would typical Burks evidence" (Ex. 1/31/22 Tr. 58). At trial, immediately before Ferguson testified, defense counsel renewed the objection and again requested a limiting instruction. The prosecutor again asserted that because the court "found it was res gestae and not Burks, I don't believe we need to read a limine instruction" (Tr. II 127). The court did not give any limiting instruction. Ferguson's testimony included allegations that Petitioner sent her unsolicited photographs of his penis and made inappropriate comments about her body. The jury received no guidance restricting its use of this evidence.

Analysis: Ferguson's testimony carried an enormous risk of unfair prejudice. Without a limiting instruction, the jury was permitted to reason that Petitioner had a character for sexual misconduct and acted in conformity with that character. The court's reasoning---that res gestae evidence categorically does not require a limiting instruction---is legally erroneous and constitutionally infirm. Under the principles articulated in Agado, the "better practice" of giving a limiting instruction was constitutionally required here due to the severe prejudice and the fact that Ferguson's claims were temporally remote from the charged offense (2017 versus late 2018). The failure to provide any safeguard against the jury's misuse of this classic propensity evidence rendered the trial fundamentally unfair.

Conclusion: The trial court's failure to give a limiting instruction for Ferguson's testimony violated due process. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 22: PREDETERMINED RULING ON NEW TRIAL MOTION

Issue: Whether the trial court violated due process by announcing a predisposition against granting a new trial before hearing any argument or evidence.

Rule: "A fair trial in a fair tribunal is a basic requirement of due process." In re Murchison, 349 U.S. 133, 136 (1955). "Prejudgment is the antithesis of a fair trial... A fair and impartial trial is a judicial process by which a court hears before it decides; by which it conducts a dispassionate inquiry and renders judgment only after receiving evidence." People v. White, 618 N.E.2d 889, 892 (Ill. App. Ct. 1993). A judge's expressed opinion on the fairness of a prior outcome has substantial probative force that such a state of mind existed during the proceeding. See State v. Nunes, 205 A.2d 24, 26 (R.I. 1964).

Only after announcing this predisposition did the court permit argument. Defense counsel then argued the motion. Petitioner also testified regarding the coerced waiver and other issues. The court denied the motion.

Analysis: The court's statement was an express declaration of prejudgment before the hearing began. Under White, this is the "antithesis of a fair trial" because the court adjudged the controversy without hearing the evidence that ought to resolve it. Under Murchison, a fair tribunal is a basic requirement of due process. The predetermined mindset infected the entire proceeding, including the court's treatment of Petitioner's testimony about the coerced waiver. When Petitioner testified, the court had already announced it saw no legal basis for relief. The denial of meaningful, open-minded review violated due process.

Conclusion: The trial court's predetermined ruling on the new-trial motion violated due process. The conviction must be vacated.

Due process requires "a procedure conducive to sentencing based on correct information." State v. Skaff, 447 N.W.2d 84, 88 (Wis. Ct. App. 1989). The "Defendant's Version" was never requested, meaning Petitioner was denied the opportunity to present his account to the sentencing court. Under Townsend and Gardner, sentencing without an opportunity to correct or explain inaccurate information denies due process. The false forensic interview date, the inclusion of uncharged and untested Ferguson allegations, and the inflammatory characterization of a prior misdemeanor all rendered the PSI fundamentally unreliable. Under Skaff and Patterson, the right to be sentenced based on correct information is fundamental. The court's express reliance on this corrupted, prosecutor-controlled report compounded the constitutional error. Conclusion: The sentence based on and imposed in reliance upon a constitutionally defective PSI violated due process and must be vacated.

GROUND 24: DEFECTIVE § 2803.1 NOTICE AND FAILURE TO STRIKE

Issue: Whether the State's filing of a materially false § 2803.1 notice---alleging disclosure at "grandmother's house"---and the trial court's failure to strike it or require a corrected notice violated due process by depriving Petitioner of adequate notice and a fair opportunity to challenge the hearsay.

Rule: An indictment or charging document must provide fair notice of the charges and allege the offense with reasonable particularity to allow the defendant to prepare a defense. Russell v. United States, 369 U.S. 749, 763-64 (1962). A defendant's right to be informed of the nature of the evidence against him is fundamental to due process. Wardius v. Oregon, 412 U.S. 470, 474 (1973). Where the State provides materially false information in a notice filing, it deprives the accused of the opportunity to confront and challenge the evidence. United States v. Bagley, 473 U.S. 667, 682 (1985). While a technical violation of a state notice rule may not create a federal claim, affirmative misrepresentations that mislead the defense violate due process. Davis v. Workman, 695 F.3d 1060, 1079 (10th Cir. 2012).

Facts: The State filed a Notice of Intent to Introduce Child Hearsay alleging that the complainant disclosed the abuse occurred "at her grandmother's house in the bedroom while she was coloring" (Ex. K4). This scenario never occurred. At the January 21, 2022 hearing, the prosecutor acknowledged: "I would like to strike that. That is an error on my part on a cut and paste to a previous case. No replacement notice was ever filed. The defense was left to challenge the hearsay based on a notice that described a scenario the State itself conceded never happened.

Analysis: The defective notice violated due process by depriving Petitioner of adequate notice. Under Russell and Wardius, notice must be specific enough to allow defense preparation. Under Bagley, the State's provision of materially false information about the context of the hearsay statement deprived the defense of a reasonable opportunity to investigate and prepare its challenge for the reliability hearing. The court's failure to strike the defective notice or require a corrected one compounded the error, forcing the § 2803.1 hearing to proceed on a false and misleading premise. Under Davis, this affirmative misrepresentation transforms a state procedural violation into a federal due process violation. The defense was left to guess at the actual circumstances it was supposed to be challenging.

Conclusion: The materially false § 2803.1 notice and the court's failure to strike it violated due process. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 25: DEFERRED AND MID-TRIAL § 2803.1 RELIABILITY RULING

Issue: Whether the trial court's deferral of its § 2803.1 reliability ruling for five months---until mid-trial, without contemporaneously viewing the forensic interview---violated due process by depriving Petitioner of meaningful adversarial testing of the State's central evidence.

Rule: The Confrontation Clause requires that gatekeeping determinations regarding the reliability of child hearsay occur before the evidence is presented to the jury to preserve the opportunity for meaningful adversarial testing. Idaho v. Wright, 497 U.S. 805, 822 (1990). Deferring admissibility rulings until mid-trial deprives counsel of the ability to adjust strategy and undermines structural fairness. Myatt v. Hannigan, 910 F.2d 680, 685 (10th Cir. 1990). The reliability determination must be a prerequisite to admission, not an afterthought. United States v. Owens, 484 U.S. 554, 561 (1988). A defendant cannot be forced to proceed through critical stages of trial without knowing whether the central evidence against him will be admitted. Crawford v. Washington, 541 U.S. 36, 68 (2004).

By this time, the defense had already delivered its opening statement, cross-examined multiple witnesses, and committed to its trial strategy---all without knowing whether the State's most critical piece of evidence would be admitted.

Analysis: The trial court's five-month deferral of its gatekeeping function violated due process. Under Idaho v. Wright, the reliability determination must be made before evidence is presented to the jury. By deferring the ruling until the third day of trial, the court stripped the defense of its ability to plan its trial strategy, including what to say in opening statements and how to cross-examine witnesses. Under Myatt, this uncertainty regarding the admissibility of key evidence fundamentally impaired the right to present a defense. The fact that the court could not even remember what proceeding the disk came from underscores the inadequacy of the review. The defense was forced to proceed through the most critical stages of trial without knowing whether the central piece of evidence would be allowed, violating the adversarial testing requirement of Crawford and Owens.

Conclusion: The deferred and mid-trial § 2803.1 reliability ruling violated due process. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 26: DEFECTIVE § 2803.1 RELIABILITY DETERMINATION

Issue: Whether the trial court's § 2803.1 reliability determination was constitutionally inadequate because the court failed to watch the forensic interview contemporaneously with the hearing, failed to make specific findings of trustworthiness tied to the video's contents, and could not recall the procedural context of the exhibit.

Rule: The admission of child hearsay under the Confrontation Clause requires specific findings of "particularized guarantees of trustworthiness" based on the totality of the circumstances surrounding the making of the statement. Idaho v. Wright, 497 U.S. 805, 819 (1990). This inquiry must be based on the circumstances that render the declarant worthy of belief and cannot be based on other corroborating evidence. Id. at 822. A reliability determination made without contemporaneous review of the evidence itself is constitutionally inadequate. United States v. Spotted War Bonnet, 933 F.2d 1471, 1477 (8th Cir. 1991). The court must make specific findings based on the totality of circumstances. State v. Redman, 916 S.W.2d 787, 791 (Mo. 1996).

Facts: The court conducted the § 2803.1 hearing on January 21, 2022, but did not watch the forensic interview video at or before the hearing. Instead, the court stated it would watch the video later (Ex. L6). The court did not watch the video until the evening of June 22, 2022---five months later, during the trial itself. When the court rendered its ruling on the morning of June 23, 2022, it could not remember what proceeding the disk came from, stating: "I'm not sure if that --- I think that was from the preliminary hearing or something" (Tr. III 5-6). The court made no specific findings of fact based on the video's contents. The court did not reference any particularized guarantees of trustworthiness. The court's entire reliability analysis consisted of: "I believe that the interview was conducted in such a way that comports with the statutory requirements that it would be admissible and be able to be shown to the jury" (Tr. III 5). No specific findings tied to the video's contents were articulated.

Analysis: The trial court's procedure rendered the reliability hearing a nullity. Under Idaho v. Wright, the court must assess reliability based on circumstances surrounding the making of the statement. Under Spotted War Bonnet, a court cannot conduct this inquiry without actually viewing the statement contemporaneously with the hearing. By separating the adversarial hearing from its review of the evidence by five months, and by reviewing the video in the middle of trial rather than as part of the gatekeeping process, the court made it impossible for the defense to meaningfully test the statement's reliability in context. The court's failure to remember which hearing the disk came from demonstrates the inadequacy of the review. Under Redman, the court's failure to make specific findings tied to the video's contents left the reliability determination without the required particularized guarantees of trustworthiness. The conclusory statement that the interview "comports with the statutory requirements" is not a sufficient reliability finding under the Confrontation Clause.

Conclusion: The defective § 2803.1 reliability determination violated the Confrontation Clause and due process. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 27: ADMISSION OF UNAUTHENTICATED SUBSTITUTE FORENSIC INTERVIEW DISK

Issue: Whether the trial court violated due process by permitting the State to publish to the jury a different forensic interview disk ("State's 6") than the one the court reviewed and admitted ("State's 1" / "Court's 1"), without requiring separate authentication or reliability findings for the substitute disk.

Rule: The admission of testimonial evidence without proper authentication violates the Confrontation Clause. Crawford v. Washington, 541 U.S. 36, 68 (2004). When the evidence shown to the jury is physically distinct from the evidence vetted by the court, foundational requirements have not been met, and the defendant is deprived of the assurance that the jury is viewing only properly admitted evidence. United States v. Gutierrez De Lopez, 761 F.3d 1123, 1137 (10th Cir. 2014). Evidence must be authenticated as a true representation of what it purports to be before it may be admitted. United States v. Cronic, 466 U.S. 648, 659 (1984). The substitution of an unvetted exhibit for one the court reviewed violates foundational evidentiary requirements and fundamental fairness. Sisk v. State, 192 A.2d 108, 110 (Md. 1963).

Facts: At the January 21, 2022 pretrial hearing, Christi Cornett authenticated a specific disk labeled "State's Exhibit 1." She testified that she "observed it earlier today and initialled and dated when I saw it" (MH Tr. 10). The court admitted it for purposes of the hearing. On June 23, 2022, the court stated it had watched "State's Exhibit 1" the previous night and ruled the interview admissible. The court requested it be relabeled "Court's Exhibit 1" and that a "different sticker" be placed on it (Tr. III 5-6). The State also referenced an agreed redaction of "like 12 seconds" related to a protective order statement. The prosecutor stated: "We have shown them the redacted copy. They've agreed to it. That's the one that we intend upon admitting through Ms. Cornett" (Tr. III 6). However, when the forensic interview was published to the jury, the State introduced "State's Exhibit 6"---a different disk (Tr. III 122). This disk had never been separately authenticated, bore no evidence of the initials and date Cornett testified she placed on the original, and was never subjected to any judicial gatekeeping review. The court conducted no additional review of the altered disk. The court admitted State's 6 over defense objection and it was played for the jury.

Analysis: The jury was shown an exhibit that had not been subjected to judicial gatekeeping. The disk the court reviewed and found reliable was State's 1/Court's 1. The disk shown to the jury was State's 6---a different, admittedly altered version containing a redaction. Under Crawford, the admission of testimonial hearsay requires strict adherence to procedural safeguards, including authentication. Under Gutierrez De Lopez, courts cannot rely on "cursory generalized statements" to admit confrontation-impairing evidence. Here, there was no separate authentication of State's 6; the witness was simply asked if it was accurate "other than" the redaction. Under Cronic, the adversarial process requires meaningful testing, which was impossible when the jury viewed a different disk than the one the court vetted. Under Sisk, the substitute disk had no independent foundation, violating due process. The court never reviewed the redacted version to confirm that the "12 seconds" removed did not affect the reliability or completeness of the evidence. Conclusion: The admission of an unauthenticated substitute forensic interview disk violated the Confrontation Clause and due process. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 28: FALSE STATEMENT "NO ONE EVER SAID 2018"

United States, 842 A.2d 690, 692 (D.C. 2004). The integrity of the evidence before the jury, not the defense's knowledge, governs the State's obligation. Paysun Long v. Pate, 386 U.S. 1, 7 (1967).

Under Napue, the State may not knowingly use false evidence or allow it to go uncorrected. Under Woodall, "the responsibility and duty to correct [a known falsehood] and elicit the truth lies with the prosecutor." Under Paysun Long (Hamilton, J., dissenting), "Napue addresses not what the defense knows but the integrity of the evidence before the jury." The jury was entitled to know the truth from the government, not to be actively misled. Under Miller, this is a due process violation requiring reversal. The statement directly affected the jury's ability to evaluate the timeline evidence and undermined the defense theory of physical impossibility.

Conclusion: The prosecutor's knowing false statement to the jury violated due process. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 29: IMPROPER VOUCHING FOR IMPEACHED COMPLAINANT

Issue: Whether the prosecutor's repeated statements vouching for the complainant's credibility---after the court issued an impeachment instruction---violated due process by placing the government's prestige behind a judicially impeached witness.

Rule: A prosecutor may not express personal belief in a witness's credibility. United States v. Young, 470 U.S. 1, 18 (1985). The test for improper vouching is whether the jury could reasonably believe the prosecutor was indicating a personal belief in the witness's credibility. United States v. Causey, 834 F.2d 1277, 1285 (6th Cir. 1988). By standing before the jury as the government's representative, the prosecutor embodies the office, and expressing personal belief places the prestige of the government behind the witness. United States v. Garcia, 758 F.3d 714, 717 (6th Cir. 2014) (concurrence). Such comments convey the impression that the prosecutor is aware of evidence unknown to the jury and induce the jury to trust the government's judgment rather than its own view of the evidence. State v. Quint, 97 Conn. App. 72, 904 A.2d 216 (2006) (dissent). Vouching is not cured simply because defense counsel attacked the witness's credibility; defense counsel does not open the door for prosecutorial vouching. People v. Rodriguez, 9 Cal. 5th 474 (2020).

Facts: The court issued an impeachment instruction for the complainant (Ex. K6). The prosecutor nonetheless stated: "We know she's telling the truth" (Tr. IV 78, 81), "She has absolutely no motive to make this up" (Tr. IV 61), "She benefits from it in no way" (Tr. IV 72), "Brianna Jackson has absolutely no motive to make this story up and no motive to lie" (Tr. IV 72), "We know Brionna is telling the truth" (Tr. IV 81), and "She experienced it. That's not a detail you can make up... It happened to her" (Tr. IV 85). The prosecutor vouched for the complainant's truthfulness at least twenty-one separate times during closing.

Analysis: The vouching directly contradicted the court's instruction that the complainant's testimony could be "not believable or truthful." Under Young, the prosecutor may not express personal belief in a witness's truthfulness. Under Causey, the test is whether the jury could reasonably believe the prosecutor was indicating personal belief---"We know she's telling the truth" satisfies this test. Under Garcia, the prosecutor "embodies the office," and placing the government's prestige behind an impeached witness is improper. Under Quint (dissent), such comments "convey the impression that the prosecutor is aware of evidence supporting charges against the defendant of which the jury has no knowledge" and "induce the jury to trust the Government's judgment rather than its own view of the evidence." The prosecutor usurped the jury's credibility function and undermined the impeachment instruction. Under Rodriguez, the defense's prior attacks on credibility do not open the door to vouching.

Conclusion: The prosecutor's improper vouching for a judicially impeached witness violated due process. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 36: IMPROPER BURDEN-MET DECLARATIONS

Issue: Whether the prosecutor violated due process by repeatedly declaring during closing argument that the State had "met its burden" or "proved" each element of the charged crimes, thereby usurping the jury's exclusive role as the arbiter of whether the State had carried its burden of proof.

Rule: The prosecutor may not express a personal opinion as to whether the State has met its burden of proof; that determination is the exclusive province of the jury. United States v. Young, 470 U.S. 1, 18 (1985). A prosecutor's statement that the government has "proved" or "met" its burden invades the province of the jury and constitutes reversible misconduct when it is pervasive and not isolated. See United States v. Modica, 663 F.2d 1173, 1178-79 (2d Cir. 1981) (prosecutor's repeated declarations that the government had "proved" its case usurped the jury's function). The test is whether the prosecutor's comments "so infected the trial with unfairness as to make the resulting conviction a denial of due process." Donnelly v. DeChristoforo, 416 U.S. 637, 643 (1974). Where the prosecutor repeatedly tells the jury that the State has already met its burden on each element, the cumulative effect is to strip the jury of its independent judgment. Berger v. United States, 295 U.S. 78, 88 (1935).

Facts: During closing argument, the prosecutor systematically walked through each element of the two charged counts and, after playing short clips from the forensic interview, declared the State's burden met on each one. For Count I, she declared: "The State of Oklahoma has met their burden of proof as it relates to that first element" (Tr. IV 19); "Right there. Right there we've met that element" (Tr. IV 20); "the State of Oklahoma has met their burden for that second element of touching and felt" (Tr. IV 21); "the State of Oklahoma has met their burden of proof as it relates to the third element, the body or private parts" (Tr. IV 21-22); "we have proved our burden of proof as it relates to that fourth element" (Tr. IV 22); "The State of Oklahoma has met their fifth element" (Tr. IV 24); "the State of Oklahoma has met their burden as it relates to the sixth element" (Tr. IV 25). For Count II, she continued: "the State of Oklahoma has demonstrated all three of these elements" (Tr. IV 27). She told the jury: "And because of that, the State of Oklahoma is going to ask that you find the defendant guilty on both counts" (Tr. IV 37). In total, the prosecutor declared the State's burden satisfied on each element at least eight separate times during closing.

Analysis: These were not arguments about what the evidence showed; they were direct assertions that the State had already proven its case. Under Young and Modica, a prosecutor may not tell the jury that the government has met its burden—that determination belongs exclusively to the jury. By walking through each element and declaring it satisfied, the prosecutor effectively instructed the jury that its task was complete before deliberations began. The repetition was not isolated but systematic and deliberate, covering every element of both counts. Under Berger and Donnelly, this pervasive usurpation of the jury's function rendered the trial fundamentally unfair. The prejudice is manifest: the jury was told, repeatedly and emphatically, that the State had already done its job, leaving the jury with the impression that its role was merely to ratify the prosecutor's conclusions rather than to independently determine whether the evidence satisfied the constitutional standard of proof beyond a reasonable doubt.

Conclusion: The prosecutor's repeated declarations that the State had met its burden on each element violated due process by usurping the jury's exclusive role. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 38: ARBITRARY CHARGING WINDOW DESPITE CONSISTENT LATE-2018 TIMELINE

The State obtained the two-year window solely because B.J. could not name an exact date (PHC Tr. 69-74).

Analysis: Every time the complainant testified, she described a temporal anchor that points to December 2018. The State exploited a child's inability to state a specific date to expand the window far beyond the only period she ever alleged, defeating Petitioner's ability to present an alibi. The preliminary hearing testimony shows she thought the wedding was "a year and a half" after they moved in, placing it after the Dec. 2018 timeframe, and trial testimony clarified and confirmed that she thinks of "before they got married" to mean the separation, not the wedding. GROUND 40: DETECTIVE JUDY'S FALSE TESTIMONY

Issue: Whether the State violated due process by knowingly presenting and failing to correct Detective Judy's false testimony that he "can't talk to her" regarding the complainant.

Rule: A conviction obtained through the State's knowing use of false testimony, or its failure to correct false testimony, violates due process. Napue v. Illinois, 360 U.S. 264, 269 (1959). The duty to correct extends to the entire prosecution office. Giglio v. United States, 405 U.S. 150, 154 (1972). The principle applies with equal force to false testimony on credibility. Napue, 360 U.S. at 269. The test is whether the false testimony "could … in any reasonable likelihood have affected the judgment of the jury." United States v. Brawer, 496 F.2d 703, 707 (2d Cir. 1974) (quoting Napue, 360 U.S. at 271).

Facts: Detective Judy, the lead investigator, testified at trial that he could not speak with the complainant: "The only witness who would have had any information would have been B.J., and I can't talk to her" (Ex. G1, Tr. III 35-36). This testimony was demonstrably false. At the July 29, 2019, VPO hearing, the complainant testified under oath that she had "talked to Detectives" and "gave statements" (Ex. C9) and even corrected the defense attorney when he left out the word "Detective" by stating "talked to Police and Detective" (Ex. C10). The State's own witness list confirmed DHS conducted interviews "along with law enforcement" (Ex. K9). The State possessed the VPO transcript but never corrected Judy's false testimony.

Analysis: Judy's false claim was the State's primary explanation for the absence of investigative follow-up. Under Napue, the State may not knowingly use false testimony "to obtain a tainted conviction," and this principle "does not cease to apply merely because the false testimony goes only to the credibility of the witness." 360 U.S. at 269. Under Giglio, the entire prosecution office is responsible for correcting known falsehoods. The State possessed the VPO transcript yet allowed Judy's lie to stand. Under Brawer, the materiality standard is a "reasonable likelihood" of affecting the jury's judgment. Here, Judy's lie about the most basic aspect of his investigation directly undermined the integrity of the State's case. Had the jury learned the lead investigator was willing to lie about his own conduct, no rational juror could have trusted the investigation or its conclusions.

Conclusion: The State's knowing use and failure to correct Detective Judy's false testimony violated due process. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 41: TIFFANY NORTON'S FALSE TESTIMONY

Issue: Whether the State violated due process by knowingly presenting and failing to correct Tiffany Norton's false testimony about the VPO filing date, her knowledge of the allegations, and her communications with the complainant.

Rule: Knowingly allowing false testimony to stand uncorrected violates due process. Napue v. Illinois, 360 U.S. 264, 269 (1959); Giglio v. United States, 405 U.S. 150, 154 (1972). The false testimony need only have a "reasonable likelihood" of affecting the jury's judgment. United States v. Meinster, 619 F.2d 1041, 1051 (4th Cir. 1980) (Sobeloff, J., dissenting) (describing standard as a "low threshold" because false testimony involves "a corruption of the truth-seeking function of the trial process").

Facts: Norton testified at trial that she filed the VPO after the forensic interview (Tr. II 60); in fact, she filed it before the interview, on July 15, 2019 (Ex. C7). She claimed she did not know the specific allegations (Tr. II 82), contradicting her earlier confirmation of the VPO details (Tr. II 60). She claimed she never spoke to the complainant about the case (Tr. II 57-58), contradicting the complainant's testimony of multiple phone calls and crying conversations (Ex. C14-C15; Forensic Interview 30:38-30:42). She denied signing divorce papers in June 2019, but Officer Pierce's report documented her statement that she had notarized divorce papers around June 27, 2019. The State never corrected any of these falsehoods.

Analysis: Norton's testimony contained multiple distinct falsehoods on material points. Under Napue, each required correction. Her false VPO date was not trivial: it allowed the State to suggest the forensic interview prompted the filing, when in fact the VPO was filed the day after the initial police disclosure—before any formal interview. Her false claim of ignorance concealed her role in shaping the complaint. Her denial of communications contradicted the complainant's sworn testimony. Under Giglio, the prosecutor's office is responsible for the truthfulness of its witnesses. The State possessed every document needed to expose these lies yet remained silent. Under Meinster, the "low threshold" is easily met: false testimony on multiple material points "corrupts the truth-seeking function" and undermines confidence in the verdict. The cumulative effect of these uncorrected lies deprived Petitioner of a fair trial.

Conclusion: The State's knowing use of false testimony from a key witness, and its failure to correct known falsehoods, violated due process. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 42: BAILEY FERGUSON'S FABRICATED TIMELINE

Issue: Whether the State violated due process by knowingly presenting and failing to correct Bailey Ferguson's fabricated work timeline, the foundation for her res gestae testimony.

Rule: A conviction obtained through the State's knowing use of false testimony, or its failure to correct false testimony, violates due process. Napue v. Illinois, 360 U.S. 264, 269 (1959). The principle applies with equal force to false testimony that goes only to credibility. Id. The State has an affirmative duty to investigate and correct false testimony once on notice of its existence. United States v. Quinn, 537 F. Supp. 2d 99, 117 (D.D.C. 2008). The test is whether the false testimony "could ... in any reasonable likelihood have affected the judgment of the jury." United States v. Brawer, 496 F.2d 703, 707 (2d Cir. 1974) (quoting Napue, 360 U.S. at 271).

Facts: Ferguson testified she worked for Petitioner through October 2018—claiming she was there for over a year and remained after her 18th birthday in October 2018 (Tr. II 131-137; Hearing Tr. 8-10). This timeline was the foundation for her res gestae claim that Petitioner made inappropriate comments "while she was babysitting" near the time of the alleged offense. Documentary evidence proved she stopped working in October 2017—one year earlier (Ex. J27, J29, J35-J36). Facebook messages showed an argument with her stepmother and Petitioner in October 2017 ending the employment relationship (Ex. J27). Further messages proved Austin's grandfather provided childcare from October 2017 through August 2018 (Ex. J29, J35, J36). Petitioner's children moved to their mother's home in August 2018 and his son returned to public school, eliminating any need for a daytime caregiver (Ex. J16-J18). The State never corrected this falsehood.

Analysis: Ferguson's fabricated timeline was the linchpin of the res gestae ruling. Under Napue, the State may not knowingly use false testimony "to obtain a tainted conviction," and this principle "does not cease to apply merely because the false testimony goes only to the credibility of the witness." 360 U.S. at 269. Under Quinn, the government's "strained dichotomy between 'knowing' and being 'highly suspicious' constitutes no excuse" when it fails to investigate and correct testimony contradicted by documents in its possession. 537 F. Supp. 2d at 117. Ferguson's claim of working through October 2018 was not a memory lapse but a year-long fabrication that enabled the res gestae admission. Without Ferguson's false timeline, her testimony was temporally remote—dating to 2017, well before any alleged conduct—and inadmissible. The res gestae evidence—the only non-complainant corroboration—was built on a lie.

Conclusion: The State's knowing use and failure to correct Ferguson's fabricated timeline violated due process. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 43: CHRISTI CORNETT'S CONTRADICTORY COACHING STATEMENTS

Issue: Whether the State violated due process by failing to correct forensic interviewer Christi Cornett's trial testimony denying any appearance of coaching, when she had previously admitted at the pretrial hearing that the complainant appeared to have been told what to expect.

Rule: The State's knowing use of false or contradictory testimony to obtain a conviction violates due process. Napue, 360 U.S. at 269. The duty to correct extends to material contradictions in an expert's testimony that bear directly on the reliability of the State's central evidence. Giglio v. United States, 405 U.S. 150, 154 (1972). The defense's ability to expose contradictions does not absolve the State of its duty to correct known falsehoods. Paysun Long v. Pfister, 874 F.3d 544, 557 (7th Cir. 2017) (Hamilton, J., dissenting) ("we do not believe that the fact that the jury was apprised of other grounds for believing that the witness … may have had an interest in testifying against petitioner turned what was otherwise a tainted trial into a fair one").

Facts: At the January 21, 2022 pretrial hearing, Cornett was asked: "Okay. So she clearly had been told or informed as to what to expect and what the purpose of the meeting with you is going to be. Is that right?" Cornett answered: "It appears that way, yes" (Ex. L1, MH Tr. 33 lines 5-8). This was an acknowledgment that the complainant showed signs of having been coached or pre-interviewed. At trial, Cornett testified to the opposite: when asked whether she had "any information in this case that you thought that someone had told Brianna what to say," she answered "No" (Ex. G10, Tr. III 119-120). She testified she saw no contradictions and no signs of coaching. The State never disclosed the pretrial admission to the jury or corrected the trial testimony.

Analysis: Cornett's contradictory testimony on coaching went to the heart of the forensic interview's reliability. The State relied on the interview as its primary evidence. The pretrial admission of apparent coaching directly supported the defense theory of contamination and outside influence. Under Napue, the State's failure to correct Cornett's trial denial—knowing it contradicted her prior sworn statement—violated due process. Under Giglio, the prosecutor's office is responsible for the truthfulness of its witnesses. Under Paysun Long, the State cannot rely on the defense's ability to expose contradictions to absolve itself of the duty to correct its own witness. The jury was left with a false impression of an untainted forensic process, and the State's silence allowed the jury to believe the expert had found no signs of coaching when she had previously acknowledged them.

Conclusion: The State's knowing presentation of contradictory expert testimony without correction violated due process. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 44:  CONTRADICTORY USE OF JEFFREY JACKSON'S TESTIMONY

Issue: Whether the State violated due process by arguing at the § 2803.1 hearing that Jackson's hearsay was reliable enough to admit, then arguing to the jury that it was unreliable due to stress and the "telephone game."

Rule: A prosecutor's interest in a criminal prosecution is not that it shall win a case, but that justice shall be done. Berger v. United States, 295 U.S. 78, 88 (1935). While a prosecutor may strike hard blows, he is not at liberty to strike foul ones. Id. The use of theories that are factually contradictory to secure a conviction violates due process. Bankhead v. State, 182 S.W.3d 253, 262 (Mo. Ct. App. 2006). The People's use of irreconcilable theories of guilt, unjustified by a good faith justification for the inconsistency, is fundamentally unfair. In re Sakarias, 35 Cal. 4th 140, 160 (2005).

Facts: At the pretrial hearing, the State argued Jackson's hearsay had "particularized guarantees of trustworthiness" and the circumstances of the disclosure were reliable. Based on those representations, the court admitted the testimony. At closing, the prosecutor argued Jackson's account was unreliable—the product of stress and a "telephone game"—and that his inconsistencies should be excused (Tr. IV 62-65). She told the jury: "So this police report you have is Brianna telling her dad and then Caitlin, and then Caitlin then talking about it, and then them calling the police, and then the police writing it down... That is one person's story on top of another, on top of another, on top of another" (Tr. IV 63).

Analysis: The State's contradictory positions deprived Petitioner of a fair evidentiary framework. Under Berger, the prosecutor's duty is to seek justice, not to manipulate evidence for tactical advantage. The State used "reliability" to secure admission, then "unreliability" to explain away damaging inconsistencies. Under Bankhead, "the use of theories that are factually contradictory to secure convictions... violates the principles of due process." 182 S.W.3d at 262. Under Sakarias, "the People's use of irreconcilable theories of guilt or culpability, unjustified by a good faith justification for the inconsistency, is fundamentally unfair, for it necessarily creates the potential for—and, where prejudicial, actually achieves—a false conviction." 35 Cal. 4th at 160. The prejudice is manifest: Jackson's testimony was the only evidence of the initial disclosure, and the State manipulated its reliability to serve whichever purpose furthered conviction.

Conclusion: The State's inconsistent arguments on Jackson's credibility violated due process. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 45: VERBATIM ADOPTION OF STATE'S BRIEF

Issue: Whether the district court violated due process by issuing a GROUND : order that was a near-verbatim copy of the State's response, containing no independent findings, no analysis, and no engagement with Petitioner's evidence.

Rule: A post-conviction order "predicated ex parte on findings and conclusions drafted by the prosecution" undermines judicial integrity and violates due process. Pederson v. State, 649 N.W.2d 161, 167 (Minn. 2002). Federal courts have held that "wholesale adoptions" of a party's memoranda "provide an inadequate and inappropriate basis for meaningful determination" and appellate review. Rudenko v. Costello, 286 F.3d 51, 62-63 (2d Cir. 2002). In the Tenth Circuit, "to dispose of a claim without considering the facts supporting it is not a decision on the merits." Wilson v. Workman, 577 F.3d 1284, 1295 (10th Cir. 2009). A state court decision grounded in procedural principles without independent analysis is not entitled to AEDPA deference. Thomas v. Warden, 786 F.3d 450, 459 (6th Cir. 2015).

Facts: On January 9, 2026, the State filed its Response to Petitioner's Application for Post-Conviction Relief. The State's brief was a seven-page document arguing procedural bars and asserting Petitioner's claims were "conclusory" and "without merit." On February 13, 2026, the district court issued its Order Denying Petitioner's Application for Post-Conviction Relief. A side-by-side comparison reveals the court's order is a near-verbatim reproduction of the State's response. The order replicates the State's language on procedural bars, the State's mischaracterization of Petitioner's claims as "conclusory," the State's false assertions that "each of Petitioner's arguments were, or could have been, raised on direct appeal," and the State's argument that Petitioner's evidence was "not new" and "not relevant." The order contains no original findings of fact, no original conclusions of law, no citations to Petitioner's 171 exhibits, and no indication the court independently reviewed the record. Petitioner had filed a Notice of Admissions on January 16, 2026, alerting the court to the State's factual falsehoods; the court adopted them anyway. The OCCA summarily affirmed.

Analysis: The verbatim order was not a judicial act; it was a clerical transcription of the State's litigation position. Under Pederson, when a post-conviction order is "predicated ex parte on findings and conclusions drafted by the prosecution," it undermines the appearance of justice. Under Rudenko, "wholesale adoptions" provide an "inadequate and inappropriate basis for meaningful determination." The Tenth Circuit in Wilson held that a court's failure to consider the evidence presented means it "has not adjudicated that claim on the merits." Here, the court adopted the State's false assertions—that Petitioner "acknowledges that his claims have been waived," that all claims were raised on direct appeal, that the evidence was "not new"—without any independent verification. The order ignored 171 exhibits, a 107-page appendix, and the undisputed timeline evidence proving physical impossibility. The OCCA's affirmance of this nullity was not a merits adjudication entitled to AEDPA deference. Petitioner was denied the independent judicial review that due process requires.

Conclusion: The verbatim adoption of the State's brief violated due process and was not a merits adjudication. The conviction must be vacated, and this Court should review all claims de novo.

GROUND 46: FACTUAL ERRORS CONTRADICTED BY RECORD

Issue: Whether the district court's denial order contained factual findings that were demonstrably false and contradicted by the record, including the false claims that Petitioner's claims were raised on direct appeal and that Petitioner acknowledged waiver.

Rule: State court factual findings "might represent an 'unreasonable determination of the facts' where... reasonable minds could not disagree that the trial court misapprehended or misstated material aspects of the record in making its finding." Cardoza v. Rock, 731 F.3d 169, 196 (2d Cir. 2013) (citing Wiggins v. Smith, 539 U.S. 510, 528 (2003)). A factual determination is unreasonable if it is "arbitrary" or if "no fairminded jurist" could agree with it. Sims v. Hyatte, 914 F.3d 1078, 1084 n.1 (7th Cir. 2018) (citing Wood v. Allen, 558 U.S. 290, 301 (2010)). A state court's refusal to credit evidence that "any reasonable adjudicator would have credited" is an unreasonable determination of the facts. Brumfield v. Cain, 576 U.S. 1, 13 (2015).

Facts: The district court's order stated: "Each of Petitioner's arguments were, or could have been, raised on direct appeal" (Order, pp. 2, 8). The order further stated: "Petitioner acknowledges that his claims have been waived" (Order, p. 4). Both statements are categorically false. Petitioner's application raised claims of ineffective assistance of appellate counsel (IAAC) under Cronic and Strickland, factual innocence, and cumulative error. IAAC claims cannot be raised on direct appeal; post-conviction is the first available forum. See 22 O.S. § 1089(D)(4)(b)(2). Factual innocence was supported by "evidence of material facts, not previously presented and heard" under 22 O.S. § 1080(D)—including lease documents, text messages, photographs, and prior sworn testimony never presented to the jury—making it unreachable on direct appeal. Petitioner never acknowledged waiver; the application explicitly invoked IAAC as "sufficient reason" under § 1086 to overcome any procedural bar. The district court also stated that Petitioner's claims were "conclusory" (Order, p. 6), a finding directly contradicted by the 171 exhibits and 107-page appendix filed in support. The OCCA repeated these errors in its summary affirmance.

Analysis: These were not mere errors—they were objectively false statements contradicted by Petitioner's own filings. Under Cardoza, a finding is unreasonable when "reasonable minds could not disagree that the trial court misapprehended or misstated material aspects of the record." The order misstated that Petitioner acknowledged waiver when he explicitly invoked IAAC as sufficient reason. It found all claims were raised on direct appeal when IAAC and factual innocence were not. Under Sims and Wood, no "fairminded jurist" could agree that a petitioner who invoked IAAC as "sufficient reason" had "acknowledged waiver." Under Brumfield, the state court's refusal to credit Petitioner's explicit procedural invocation—evidence any reasonable adjudicator would have credited—was unreasonable. The false findings formed the sole basis for the application of procedural bars.

Conclusion: The district court's factually false denial order violated due process. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 47: DISMISSAL OF DOCUMENTED CLAIMS AS "CONCLUSORY"

Issue: Whether the district court's dismissal of Petitioner's 171 exhibits and 107-page appendix as "conclusory" and "bald assertions" was an unreasonable determination of facts in violation of due process.

Rule: A state court's dismissal of evidence-backed claims as "conclusory" is permissible only when the allegations are "truly bare and contradicted by the record, not when the record itself contains the evidence the court allegedly ignored." Gray v. Zook, 806 F.3d 783, 796 (4th Cir. 2015). A factual determination is unreasonable if "no fairminded jurist could conclude the determination was, in fact, correct." Smith v. Aldridge, 904 F.3d 874, 882 (10th Cir. 2018) (citing Brumfield v. Cain, 576 U.S. 1, 13 (2015)). A state court's refusal to grant an evidentiary hearing or credit evidence "that any reasonable adjudicator would have credited" is an unreasonable determination of the facts. Brumfield, 576 U.S. at 13.

Facts: Petitioner submitted 171 exhibits with his application, including 102 pages of transcript excerpts, 19 appeal documents, 16 pages of VPO transcript, and 36 photographs and messages. The exhibits documented: trial counsel's on-record admission of lack of preparation (Ex. G12), the coerced waiver colloquy (Ex. L2-L3), the prosecutor's false statement "No one ever said 2018" (Ex. H2), the State's knowing use of false testimony from Detective Judy, Tiffany Norton, Bailey Ferguson, and Christi Cornett (Ex. C9-C10, G1, J27-J29, L1, G10), the destruction of exculpatory BWC evidence (Ex. A5, F17-F18, G3-G6), and the complete breakdown of representation at sentencing where counsel branded his own client a liar (Ex. I1-I10). The 107-page appendix catalogued over 265 instances of prosecutorial misconduct with specific page and line citations. The district court dismissed all of this as "conclusory" without addressing a single exhibit. The OCCA affirmed.

Analysis: Under Gray, a court may dismiss claims as "conclusory" only when the allegations are "truly bare and contradicted by the record." Here, the record contained 171 exhibits with specific transcript citations, photographs, and messages. The allegations were not bare; they were documented. Under Smith (Tenth Circuit), "no fairminded jurist" could conclude that 171 exhibits and a 107-page appendix with specific citations are "conclusory." The court did not merely reach a different conclusion—it failed to engage with the evidence at all. The court's dismissal of a coerced waiver claim supported by a contemporaneous letter and transcript, an unpreparedness claim supported by counsel's own on-record admission, and an innocence claim supported by documentary proof of physical impossibility as "conclusory" defies logic.

Conclusion: The district court's dismissal of documented claims as "conclusory" violated due process. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 48: FAILURE TO HOLD EVIDENTIARY HEARING

Issue: Whether the district court violated due process by denying Petitioner's post-conviction application without an evidentiary hearing despite material factual disputes supported by 171 exhibits.

Rule: A federal habeas petitioner is entitled to an evidentiary hearing if he asserts a factual basis that, if true, would entitle him to relief, and he was diligent in developing the claim in state court. Boyle v. McKune, 544 F.3d 1132, 1135 (10th Cir. 2008). A hearing is constitutionally required when a petitioner alleges facts that are not refuted by the record and would entitle him to relief. Townsend v. Sain, 372 U.S. 293, 312 (1963). Oklahoma law independently mandates a hearing when material facts are disputed. 22 O.S. § 1084 ("When material facts are disputed, the court shall conduct an evidentiary hearing").

Facts: Petitioner's application presented claims supported by 171 exhibits and a 107-page appendix. These exhibits created material factual disputes on claims of coerced waiver of the right to testify, trial counsel's conflict of interest and public contradiction of Petitioner at sentencing, the State's bad-faith destruction of exculpatory evidence, the State's knowing use of false testimony, appellate counsel's constructive denial of counsel, and Petitioner's factual innocence based on documentary proof of physical impossibility. The State's response did not contest any of these material facts; it relied exclusively on procedural arguments. Under Rule 13(e) of the Rules for District Courts of Oklahoma, the State's failure to dispute material facts constituted an admission. The district court denied the application without holding an evidentiary hearing, without examining the exhibits, and without making independent factual findings. Its order was a verbatim copy of the State's proposed order.

Analysis: Under Townsend, when a petitioner alleges facts that, if proven, would entitle him to relief, a hearing is required. Under Boyle, Petitioner was entitled to a hearing because he alleged facts "not refuted by the record and would entitle him to relief." The 171 exhibits created clear factual disputes that could not be resolved on the record alone—particularly the claims of coerced waiver, which required credibility determinations between Petitioner and his former counsel, and the claims of factual innocence, which required evaluation of documentary evidence never presented to the jury. Under Oklahoma law, an evidentiary hearing is mandatory when material facts are disputed. 22 O.S. § 1084. By refusing to hold a hearing, the court denied Petitioner any opportunity to prove his factual allegations. The OCCA's affirmance of this procedural failure was unreasonable under § 2254(d)(1).

Conclusion: The denial of an evidentiary hearing violated due process. Petitioner is entitled to an evidentiary hearing in this Court, and the conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 49: FAILURE TO DISQUALIFY JUDGE BALKMAN

Issue: Whether Judge Balkman's refusal to recuse himself from adjudicating claims that directly challenged his own judicial conduct violated due process.

Rule: A judge cannot impartially rule on claims alleging his own constitutional errors. Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co., 556 U.S. 868, 886 (2009) ("An objective inquiry requires recusal when the probability of actual bias on the part of the judge is too high to be constitutionally tolerable"). "A fair trial in a fair tribunal is a basic requirement of due process." In re Murchison, 349 U.S. 133, 136 (1955) ("No man can be a judge in his own case"). A constitutionally intolerable risk of actual bias exists when the same person serves as both the subject of a constitutional challenge and the adjudicator of that challenge. Williams v. Pennsylvania, 136 S. Ct. 1899, 1905 (2016).

Facts: Petitioner moved to disqualify Judge Balkman on August 11, 2025. The motion detailed Judge Balkman's personal involvement in multiple claims raised in the application: the failure to conduct a proper waiver colloquy, the predetermined ruling on the new-trial motion, the failure to correct the prosecutor's false statement to the jury, the failure to give a limiting instruction for Ferguson's testimony, the admission of an unauthenticated substitute forensic interview disk, the unconstitutional PSI, and the verbatim adoption of the State's brief. Judge Balkman denied the motion on February 4, 2026, in a two-page order dismissing Petitioner's claims as "bare allegations." He then proceeded to rule on the application, denying all claims in the verbatim order issued nine days later. No other judge reviewed the disqualification motion. The OCCA affirmed without addressing the disqualification issue.

Analysis: The principle that no person should be judge in his own cause is fundamental to due process. Under Caperton, an objective inquiry requires recusal when the probability of actual bias is too high to be constitutionally tolerable. Under Murchison, "no man can be a judge in his own case." Under Williams, there is an impermissible risk of bias when a judge who was personally involved in critical decisions is later asked to review the propriety of those decisions. Here, the application directly challenged Judge Balkman's own conduct at trial and at the sentencing/new-trial hearing. He was asked to rule on whether his own waiver colloquy was constitutionally adequate, whether his own PSI was unconstitutional, and whether his own failure to correct the prosecutor's false statement violated due process. His personal interest in the outcome of claims challenging his own judicial integrity created a constitutionally intolerable conflict requiring disqualification. The subsequent verbatim adoption of the State's brief—without independent analysis—confirms the very bias the disqualification motion sought to prevent.

Conclusion: Judge Balkman's failure to disqualify himself violated due process. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 50: FAILURE TO ISSUE FINDINGS OF FACT AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW

Issue: Whether the district court's failure to issue any findings of fact or conclusions of law—as required by OCCA Rule 5.2(A) and 22 O.S. § 1084—violated due process and frustrated meaningful appellate review.

Rule: A state court's failure to comply with its own procedural rules does not, in itself, raise a federal constitutional claim, but where that failure results in a decision that is fundamentally arbitrary or prevents a petitioner from obtaining meaningful appellate review, it can violate due process. Wilson v. Sellers, 138 S. Ct. 1188, 1192 (2018) ("Federal courts 'look through' unexplained state-court decisions, which are not entitled to AEDPA deference"). Oklahoma law mandates that a court "shall make specific findings of fact, and state expressly its conclusions of law, relating to each issue presented." 22 O.S. § 1084. "Fundamental fairness requires that the district court enter specific findings of fact and conclusions of law relating to each material issue." DeLozier v. State, 2007 OK CR 34, ¶ 56, 173 P.3d 78, 79. A decision that contains no reasoning or factual findings is not an "adjudication on the merits" entitled to deference. Wilson, 138 S. Ct. at 1192.

Facts: The district court's February 13, 2026 order contained zero original findings of fact and zero original conclusions of law. It was a near-verbatim copy of the State's response. It did not identify which materials were considered, did not explain why any claim was rejected, did not cite a single exhibit, did not apply any legal standard to any factual allegation, and did not provide any reasoning for its conclusions. The order simply declared the claims "barred" and "without merit" without analysis. The OCCA was presented with a judicial nullity rather than a reasoned decision, and it affirmed without addressing the structural defect.

Analysis: The complete absence of findings rendered meaningful appellate review impossible. Under Wilson, such an unexplained decision is not entitled to AEDPA deference, and this Court must review the claims de novo. Under DeLozier, the failure to provide specific findings relating to each material issue is structural error. The OCCA's rules require findings of fact and conclusions of law precisely to enable meaningful review. Here, there was nothing to review—no explanation of why 171 exhibits were insufficient, no reasoning on why the innocence claim failed, no analysis of the IAAC claim under either Cronic or Strickland. The state court's failure to follow its own mandatory procedural rule resulted in a fundamentally arbitrary outcome that deprived Petitioner of a fair post-conviction process. The order is a legal nullity that cannot serve as the basis for denying relief.

Conclusion: The district court's failure to issue findings of fact and conclusions of law violated due process. The conviction must be vacated.

GROUND 1: PETITIONER HAS MADE A TRULY PURSUASIVE SHOWING OF ACTUAL INNOCENCE - UNDER House v. Bell, 547 U.S.

Issue: Whether Petitioner has presented a truly persuasive demonstration of actual innocence that independently warrants habeas relief, given that no reasonable juror, after considering all the old and new evidence, could find him guilty beyond a reasonable doubt.

Rule: The Supreme Court has “assume[d], without deciding, that a truly persuasive demonstration of ‘actual innocence’ in an extraordinary case could independently warrant habeas relief.” House v. Bell, 547 U.S. 518, 554–55 (2006); McQuiggin v. Perkins, 569 U.S. 383, 392 (2013) (same). The Tenth Circuit instructs courts to make a “holistic judgment about all the evidence, old and new, admissible and inadmissible,” in determining whether “no reasonable juror would have found the petitioner guilty beyond a reasonable doubt.” Fontenot v. Crow, 4 F.4th 982, 1031–32 (10th Cir. 2021). Under this standard, a petitioner must show that in light of new evidence, it is more likely than not that no reasonable juror would convict. While the standard is rigorous, it is met when the new evidence proves that the crime could not have occurred as alleged and the state’s case is irreparably undermined.

Facts: As detailed in the Schlup gateway section below, the prosecution’s entire case rests on a timeline that the complainant herself, in every statement prior to trial, placed in late 2018. The initial police report of July 14, 2019 documented the occurrence between November 29, 2018, and December 25, 2018, and noted that the complainant “did NOT touch Dallas penis” (Ex. A1-A3). In the forensic interview, she said it happened “before they got married” (Ex. B3) and denied penetration (“he didn’t actually go inside inside,” Ex. B9). At the VPO hearing, she said “before last Christmas” and “months ago” (Ex. C3, C5-C7). At the preliminary hearing, she placed it in the “middle of fifth grade,” “certainly not … in fourth grade,” “a long time after moving in,” and “about medium time before” the Christmas trip to Arizona (Ex. D4, D10-D11, D14). At trial, she clarified that her reference to “before they got married” meant the late-2018 separation—not the June 2018 wedding—because that is when Petitioner was with Shyanne Pike (Ex. F4). She never identified any other timeframe.

Documentary evidence—none of it presented to the jury—proves that the alleged crime could not have occurred during that window. Petitioner signed a new lease on September 4, 2018 (Ex. J1). A photograph from September 8, 2018, shows a moving truck at the new residence (Ex. J3). County assessor records confirm the address (Ex. J2). Text messages state, “I’m in the middle of moving” (Ex. J4-J5). Petitioner did not return to the home until December 30, 2018 (Ex. J6). The complainant left for Arizona on December 22, 2018 (Ex. J7) and returned on January 5, 2019 (Ex. J8). Tiffany Norton, the State’s own witness, testified that Petitioner “never spent the night” during the separation and moved back in January 2019 (Ex. F13). There was zero overlap between Petitioner’s and the complainant’s presence in the home during the only timeframe she ever alleged.

Real-time behavioral evidence contradicts the claim of abuse. The complainant’s mother reported on November 21, 2018, that “she says she misses u all the time and ask to go over there all the time” (Ex. J12) and on October 20, 2018, “she wanted to go cause she missed you” (Ex. J13). She went fishing with Petitioner and had fun (Ex. J13, J19), sent affectionate messages (“I miss you daddy,” “Love you” (Ex. J19, J20, J37, J38)), and continued normal bonding activities—dances, skating, painting nails, and a photo in Petitioner’s coveralls captioned “Look I’m Dad” on May 3, 2019 (Ex. J14-J15, J21-J26). This behavior is clinically incompatible with the trauma of sexual abuse by a caregiver.

Trial counsel possessed this exculpatory evidence (Ex. J31-J34) but never presented it, instead abandoning the alibi defense after promising it in opening statement. He coerced Petitioner’s waiver of the right to testify (Ex. L2-L3, I1-I3). The jury never saw the documentary proof of impossibility or the behavioral evidence.

In addition, the State’s case was infected by the knowing use of false testimony from multiple witnesses, including Detective Judy (who lied about not talking to the complainant, Ex. G1 vs. Ex. C9-C10), Bailey Ferguson (whose entire work history was fabricated, Ex. J27, J29), and Christi Cornett (who reversed her pretrial admission of coaching, Ex. L1 vs. Ex. G10). The prosecutor told the jury a direct lie in closing: “No one ever said 2018” (Ex. H2). The court gave OUJI-CR 9-20 impeachment instructions for both the complainant and her father (Ex. K6-K7). No physical or forensic evidence was ever introduced.

Analysis: Under House, McQuiggin, and Fontenot, this Court must make a “holistic judgment” of all the evidence. The new evidence—documentary proof of Petitioner’s absence, the complainant’s own consistent timeline, and the objective behavioral evidence—is not merely impeaching; it demonstrates that the crime could not have happened. The timeline the complainant herself provided makes the charged acts physically impossible. The State’s remaining evidence was contaminated by perjury, fabricated testimony, and prosecutorial misconduct. Even the trial court deemed the two main witnesses’ testimony so unreliable that it issued impeachment instructions. When all the evidence, old and new, is considered together, it is more likely than not that no reasonable juror would convict.

This is the “extraordinary case” the Supreme Court contemplated in House and McQuiggin. The miscarriage of justice is manifest. Petitioner’s continued incarceration violates the due process and Eighth Amendment principles recognized in those decisions. Because the state courts’ denial of relief was both contrary to and an unreasonable application of these federal standards, habeas relief must be granted.

Conclusion: Petitioner has made a truly persuasive demonstration of actual innocence under House, McQuiggin, and Fontenot. The conviction is a fundamental miscarriage of justice and must be vacated.

GROUND 2: PETITIONER IS ACTUALLY INNOCENT - UNDER SCHLUP v. DELO

Issue: Whether Petitioner has satisfied the Schlup actual innocence gateway so that any procedural default of his other constitutional claims is excused to prevent a fundamental miscarriage of justice.

Rule: Under Schlup v. Delo, 513 U.S. 298 (1995), a petitioner who presents new, reliable evidence of actual innocence may overcome procedural bars if he shows that “it is more likely than not that no reasonable juror would have convicted him in light of the new evidence.” Id. at 327. The court must consider “all the evidence, old and new, admissible and inadmissible,” House v. Bell, 547 U.S. 518, 538 (2006), and make a “holistic judgment.” Fontenot v. Crow, 4 F.4th 982, 1031 (10th Cir. 2021). The gateway applies to claims of “factual innocence, not legal innocence.” Bousley v. United States, 523 U.S. 614, 623 (1998). It exists because “principles of comity and finality must yield to the imperative of correcting a fundamentally unjust incarceration.” Schlup, 513 U.S. at 320–21.

Facts: As detailed in the freestanding claim above and throughout this petition, Petitioner has presented substantial new evidence that was never presented to the jury. That evidence includes:

· The complainant’s consistent pretrial statements locking the event to late 2018 (Ex. A1-A3, B3, C3-C7, D4-D14).
· Documentary proof that Petitioner was absent from the home from September 8, 2018, until December 30, 2018 (Ex. J1-J6).
· Proof that the complainant was in Arizona from December 22, 2018, to January 5, 2019 (Ex. J7-J8).
· Real-time messages and photos showing the complainant’s continuing affectionate behavior toward Petitioner throughout the alleged timeframe (Ex. J12-J15, J19-J26).
· Emails confirming trial counsel possessed this evidence but never used it (Ex. J31-J34), and the record of his coerced waiver of Petitioner’s right to testify (Ex. L2-L3, I1-I3).

The trial record itself contains impeachment of the State’s two primary witnesses (Ex. K6-K7), false testimony from multiple witnesses, the prosecutor’s knowing false statement (Ex. H2), and the destruction of the BWC recording of the initial disclosure (Ex. A5, F17-F18, G3-G6).

Analysis: This new evidence, considered alongside the trial record, makes it more likely than not that no reasonable juror would convict. The complainant’s own repeated, unwavering timeline places the crime in a window when Petitioner could not have been present. The documentary evidence is objective and compelling. The behavioral evidence directly contradicts the allegation of abuse. When a jury, unaware of this evidence, was left with only the evolving, impeached, and contradictory testimony of the State’s witnesses, the verdict is fatally unreliable.

The new evidence satisfies Schlup’s requirement of reliability—it consists of contemporaneous documents, photographs, text messages, and sworn testimony. It was not presented at trial because of counsel’s ineffectiveness and coercion. Under Fontenot, evidence is “new” if not presented at trial, regardless of when it was discovered. 4 F.4th at 1032. The state courts’ failure to recognize this gateway, and their insistence on procedural bars without considering the actual innocence evidence, was contrary to and an unreasonable application of Schlup, House, and McQuiggin.

Conclusion: Petitioner has satisfied the actual innocence gateway under Schlup. All procedural bars to his claims—including ineffective assistance of trial counsel, Napue and Brady/Youngblood violations, prosecutorial misconduct, coerced waiver of the right to testify, and conflict of interest—are excused. This Court must reach the merits of those claims and, for the reasons set forth in this petition, grant the writ of habeas corpus. The continued incarceration of an innocent man is a fundamental miscarriage of justice that the Constitution will not tolerate.
